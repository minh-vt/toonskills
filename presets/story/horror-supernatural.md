# Horror & Supernatural · Director's Narrative Techniques Skill Pack

## Introduction

This skill pack provides a complete set of directorial narrative technique references for the **Horror & Supernatural** genre, covering the entire workflow from macro narrative planning to micro storyboard execution. It is applicable to any visual style.

## Core Philosophy

- **The Unknown is Terror** — Real horror comes from "the unseen"; the audience's imagination is far more terrifying than any visual image.
- **Cracks in the Mundane** — The most effective horror is not the entity itself, but the "wrongness" that appears within a familiar environment.
- **Gradual Erosion** — Fear is not a one-time scare; it is a subtle unease that slowly spreads until there is nowhere left to run.
- **Human Nature is Scarier than Ghosts** — The deepest horror stems from the dark side of humanity; the supernatural is merely a mirror reflecting it.

# Narrative Technique · Horror & Supernatural · Reference Guide

---

## I. Themes and Emotional Core

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Essentials

- **The Unknown is Fear** — True horror stems from "the unseen." Do not rush to reveal the monster/ghost in its entirety; the audience’s imagination is far more terrifying than any design. The theme should revolve around "unknown threats" rather than "known monsters."
- **Cracks in the Mundane** — The most effective horror lies in "off" details appearing within a familiar, daily routine: a clock running backward, an extra chair at the table, an extra person in a photograph. The theme should emphasize the "collapse of a sense of security."
- **Slow Erosion** — Horror is not a one-time scare, but the continuous accumulation of unease. The thematic design should feature an erosion arc "from subtle to out-of-control," leading the audience from "something feels wrong" to "there is no way out."
- **Suggested Exit Emotions** — Lingering fear / Chills down the spine / Creepiness upon reflection / Fear of looking back. Avoid purely sensory triggers like "disgust" or "gore."
- **People are Scarier than Ghosts** — In high-level horror narratives, supernatural elements are merely metaphors for the dark side of humanity. The true source of fear should point to the human heart: greed, suspicion, betrayal, and madness.

---

## II. Narrative Structure and Pacing

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Essentials

- **Accumulating Unease is Fundamental** — The core task of the first half of a horror narrative is to "accumulate unease." Use minor anomalies in everyday scenes to erode the audience's sense of security bit by bit, with each instance of "wrongness" escalating in intensity.
- **Alternating Tension to Create Psychological Contrast** — Do not keep the tension constant, or the audience will become numb. Insert "false security" between horror sequences (where characters think the danger has passed) to let the audience breathe—only to tighten the tension sharply afterward.
- **Information Control is the Engine** — The essence of horror is information asymmetry: the audience doesn't know what is there, or the characters don't know the danger is already approaching. Narrative planning must explicitly state what "the audience knows" and "what the character knows" for each segment.
- **Delayed Revelation** — Key horror elements (the true form of the monster, the truth, the source of the threat) should be delayed as much as possible. Reveal a little at a time, creating a spiral of "the more you know, the more afraid you become."
- **The Climax is the Confirmation of Fear** — All the unease accumulated earlier is confirmed during the climax when the "worst premonition comes true." The climax is not the scariest moment, but the terrifying epiphany of "so that’s why... all this time."
- **Blurring Reality and Illusion** — A sophisticated technique in horror is to make the audience doubt whether what they see is real or a hallucination: Reality → Anomaly → Doubt → Confirmation → Greater Doubt. The blurrier the line between true and false, the stronger the sense of unease.

---

## III. Scene-by-Scene Mood Design

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Essentials

- **Use Somatic Language for Emotional Goals** — Don't just say "scared"; say "a sudden chill down the back" or "the urge to turn around but being too afraid to." Somatic descriptions better guide narrative pacing and performance design.
- **Typical Emotional Segments and Design** —

| Segment Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Sound Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Establishing Routine | Safety + Warmth | A normal life scene, deliberately creating an "all is well" vibe | Natural ambient sounds, relaxed atmosphere |
| Subtle Anomaly | Faint Unease | A detail that shouldn't exist; the character misses it, but the audience sees it | An uncoordinated sound mixed into ambient noise |
| Security Collapse | Seeds of Fear | Frequency of anomalies increases; the character begins to notice something is "wrong" | Ambient sounds thin out, low frequencies fade in |
| False Security | Brief Relief | Crisis seems averted; the character relaxes | Return to normal ambient sounds, music stops |
| Confirmation | Extreme Fear | The worst guess is confirmed; nowhere to run | Sudden silence or extreme low-frequency pressure |
| Lingering/Aftermath | Creepiness | It seems over, but an "off" detail remains | Previous anomalous sounds buried in daily ambient noise |

- **"Stripping Away Security" is the Core Tool** — Map the escalation of fear by having the character’s "safe zones" gradually eroded:
  - **Early Stage**: Threat is in the distance, outside, in the dark.
  - **Middle Stage**: Threat enters the living space but can still be avoided.
  - **Late Stage**: Threat is right beside you, behind you, or inside you; nowhere to escape.
- **Space is Psychology** — Use spatial characteristics to convey fear. Enclosed space = nowhere to run; Open space = nowhere to hide; Alienated familiar space = collapse of security.
- **The "Ineffability" of Fear** — The most terrifying things should not be fully described. Character reactions (dilated pupils, loss of speech, turning to run) are more effective than directly showing "the thing."

---

## IV. Sound and Music Direction

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Essentials

- **Silence is the Strongest Tool** — The ace of horror films is not a loud scream, but the absolute silence preceding it. Key horror segments should be designed as "sound vacuums"—all ambient noise suddenly vanishes, causing the audience to instinctively hold their breath.
- **Low-Frequency Pressure** — Sounds in the 18-20Hz range (infrasound), which the human ear cannot clearly hear but the body can feel, induce physiological unease. Use low-frequency noise floors during buildup; the audience won't be able to name why, but they will feel uncomfortable.
- **Unknown Sound Sources** — The golden rule of horror sound design is "hear it, but don't know what makes it." Footsteps, breathing, scratching—the more mundane the sound, the more terrifying it is when it appears where it shouldn't.
- **Ambient Sound as a Fear Thermometer** — Mark fear levels through layers of ambient sound: Normal (birds/insects/voices) → Thinning (sounds gradually fade) → Dead Silence → Anomalous sound appears.
- **Music Emotional Progression Model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Routine Safety | Normal soundtrack or pure ambient | Low |
| Seeds of Unease | Dissonant intervals, very low volume | Low-Mid |
| Fear Escalation | Low-frequency noise floor increases, rhythmic suggestions | Mid |
| Fear Climax | Sudden silence or extreme sound design | Extreme |
| False Security | Restore normal ambient sound | Low |
| Lingering Aftermath | Anomalous elements mixed into daily sounds | Low-Mid |

- **Exercise Restraint with "Jump Scares"** — Sudden loud noises (Jump Scares) should be used no more than 1-2 times, and only after a long accumulation of silence. Overusing jump scares is a low-level technique that dissolves the atmospheric dread built up previously.

---

## V. Composition and Shot Size Narrative

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Essentials

- **Narrative Functions of Three Core Compositions** —
  - **Large Negative Space** — Empty areas = potential threat zones; the audience will instinctively "search" the blank space for the source of fear.
  - **Framing/Masking** — Door cracks, windows, the end of a hallway. Limiting the view = limiting information = magnifying fear.
  - **Imbalanced Composition** — The subject is pushed to one side, leaving an "unsafe" space on the other, implying something is there.
- **Restricted Vision Equals Fear** — In horror, the less the audience sees, the more afraid they are. Use shot size and composition to control information: don't let the audience see behind the character, into corners of the room, or the full picture in the dark.
- **Threat of Spatial Depth** — Use depth to suggest "something is in the distance": a blurry silhouette at the end of a hallway, something moving in the background that shouldn't be, an unusual shape out of focus.
- **Shot Size and Information Control** — Close-up = character doesn't know what's happening around them (neither does the audience); Long shot = character is exposed to the threat (audience sees it, but the character doesn't). Use switching to create different types of fear.
- **Fear Function of Extreme Close-ups** — Extreme close-ups (pupils, lips, fingers) capture "physiological reactions to fear": dilated pupils, trembling lips, nails digging into palms.
- **"The Extra Thing"** — A core technique in horror composition: elements appearing in the frame that shouldn't be there (a second face in a mirror, a silhouette in the shadows, an extra figure in the background). The fear when the audience discovers these is far greater than any sound effect.

---

## VI. Camera Movement and Pacing

### Horror & Supernatural Narrative Essentials

- **Stillness = Oppression** — Over 70% of shots in a horror narrative should be static. A static image prevents the audience from gaining the sense of security that "someone is in control," creating the lonely fear of being "abandoned on the scene."
- **Very Slow Push-in = Approaching/Inevitability** — A push-in slower than normal suggests "something is closing in" or "you must face this." Suitable for confirmation segments.
- **Slow Pull-out = Exposure/Revealing the Whole Picture** — Slowly pulling out reveals the full horror the character hasn't seen (the silhouette behind them, the anomaly in the room). This is a classic "audience knows, character doesn't" technique.
- **Sudden Stillness** — A moving camera suddenly stopping suggests "something made it stop," creating intense unease.
- **"Breathing" Camera Movement** — Subtle, slight shaking or rising/falling to simulate a subjective "voyeuristic" feel—like someone is watching secretly. This is not handheld shaking, but a subtle "breath."
- **Movement as Threat Direction** — The direction of camera movement suggests the source of the threat. The audience instinctively assumes the camera is moving toward something it "is about to see"—you can play into this expectation or subvert it to create a scare.

# Storyboard Narrative Techniques · Horror & Supernatural · Technical Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the core tool for a director to translate a script into cinematic language. The fields in the table should be defined by the director based on project needs (e.g., shot number, shot size, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting/shadow, emotion, transitions, etc.). The following provides technical references specific to the horror and supernatural narrative genre.

---

## II. Shot Size Selection

- **Shot Size as Information Control** — The core logic of shot selection in horror is "controlling how much the audience knows." Close-ups = limited character perspective (the audience is as blind to the surroundings as the character); Long shots = characters exposed to the unknown (the audience sees danger, but the character does not).
- **The Enclosed Fear of Close-ups** — Close-ups/Extreme close-ups turn everything outside the frame into an "unknown zone," instinctively triggering audience anxiety about "what is just outside the frame." Use close-ups frequently during fear-building sequences.
- **The Exposure Fear of Long Shots** — Long shots make characters look small, isolated, and exposed, ideal for sequences where "the threat is nearby but the character is unaware."
- **Avoid the "Safety" of Medium Shots** — Medium shots provide the most "security" (moderate information and distance). In horror, minimize medium shots; instead, use extreme shifts between close-ups and long shots to create unease.
- **Establishers Must Create Unease** — Establishing shots should do more than just set the scene; they should embed a sense that "something is wrong"—blurred shapes at the frame edges, windows that shouldn't be lit, or spaces that are unnervingly still.

---

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Static by Default, Static as Oppression** — Over 70% of shots should be static. In horror, static is not "the absence of movement," but an active effort to create the suffocating feeling of being "trapped in this viewpoint."
- **Ultra-slow Push-in = Approaching** — Use a speed half as fast as a normal slow push-in to imply that "something is slowly creeping closer" or "you are forced to look closely at this."
- **Slow Pull-back = Revealing the Horror** — Slowly pull back from a character or detail to reveal a truth they haven't seen (someone standing behind them, the room is destroyed, the surrounding area is full of anomalies).
- **Sudden Stop** — A moving shot that abruptly halts suggests "something has made everything freeze," providing a powerful psychological shock.
- **Micro-breath Jitter** — Extremely subtle screen fluctuations that simulate the presence of a "voyeur" in a subjective POV shot. This is not handheld shaking, but a subtle subconscious sensation that "someone is watching."

---

## IV. Duration Control

- **Extend Fear Shots** — The duration of shots during uneasy/fearful sequences should be 50% longer than average. "Force" the audience to linger on an unsettling image, waiting for a potential horror event.
- **Allow Time for Silence** — Silent shots (vacuum of ambient sound) should last at least 3-4 seconds to allow the oppressive weight of the silence to fully impact the audience.
- **Jump Scares Should Be Extremely Short** — Keep Jump Scare-style shots to 1-1.5 seconds; cut away before the brain has time to react, leaving only the "aftershock" of fear.
- **Close-ups Last 3-4 Seconds** — Close-ups of fearful expressions need longer dwell times to let the audience "infect" themselves with the character's terror.
- **Long Shots/Empty Frames 5-7 Seconds** — Long shots and empty frames used for atmosphere need more time to allow the audience to "search" the screen for anomalies.
- **The Golden Rule: Fear Comes from Waiting** — The audience knows something terrible will happen but doesn't know when; every second of waiting amplifies the fear.

---

## V. Shot Merging Strategy (Removing AI-Feel)

- **Maintain Continuous Unease** — Shots designed to build a sense of unease should be merged into long takes whenever possible. Once broken (hard cut to a new angle), the accumulation of anxiety is reset.
- **One-Take Environmental Exploration** — When a character explores an unknown space (entering a dark room, walking down a hallway), try to use a "one-shot" approach. Fragmented cuts destroy the immersion of "experiencing it alongside the character."
- **Prioritize Narrative Density** — Every shot must advance the fear or the narrative. Purely decorative atmospheric shots should be merged into shots that have narrative functionality.
- **Director's Mindset Check** — After finishing the storyboard, self-reflect: Is the horror atmosphere continuous, or is it broken by fragments? If a scene can be achieved in a single cohesive take, do not break it into fragments.

---

## VI. One-Take (Long Take Merging)

- **Conditions for Use** — Character exploration of unknown spaces, chase/escape sequences, or the transition from safety to fear. The value of a long take in horror is that it "gives the audience no room to breathe."
- **Typical Scenes** — Character walking alone through a dark hallway, opening a door to explore a room, moving from a normal space to an abnormal one, or chases.
- **Annotation Method** — Specify the full path in the camera movement field (e.g., "One-take: Follow character through the hallway → slow push into the room → end on a static shot of the character's back"). Describe the start and end frames in the visual description.
- **Relaxed Duration** — Because horror long takes continuously build pressure, they may exceed the 6s single-shot limit, but should not exceed 15s.
- **Suffocation Control** — The longer the one-take, the stronger the audience's anxiety regarding "waiting for the horror to happen." The long take itself is a horror-generating machine.

---

## VII. Character Interaction Design

- **Solo Scenes are Horror's Stronghold** — The most effective sequences in horror often involve characters alone. The ratio of solo shots in the storyboard should be higher than group scenes.
- **Reaction is More Important than the Source** — It is not important what the character sees; it is important how they react. The storyboard should prioritize shots of "the character's fearful reaction" over shots of "the horror entity itself."
- **Restrained Action** — People in fear do not make large, sweeping movements; they freeze, curl up, or back away slowly. Avoid writing "character screams and runs away" in the storyboard; write "character freezes, fingers clenching unconsciously."

---

## VIII. Dialogue and Negative Space

- **Speak Less, Fear More** — Dialogue in horror sequences should be minimal. The more a character talks, the more "control" they feel; silence allows fear to spread.
- **Fragmented Dialogue** — Dialogue during fear should be fragmented: half-sentences, a name they can't quite shout, a whispered "Don't move." Complete sentences destroy the atmosphere of horror.
- **Audience Knows, Character Doesn't** — The best use of negative space in horror is "the audience sees it, but the character is still talking normally." Use the character's ignorance to contrast with the horror.

---

## IX. Transition Design

- **Default to Hard Cuts** — Within the same scene, use hard cuts between shots to keep things clean, crisp, and tense.
- **No Buffer Between Horror Sequences** — Unlike other genres, transitions between horror sequences should avoid giving the audience "room to rest," unless it is a deliberately staged "false sense of security."
- **The Fear of Blackout** — Use a brief black frame (0.5-1s) as a transition; during the blackout, the audience sees nothing = peak fear. Do not over-rely on this.
- **J-Cuts (Audio-First Transitions)** — The visual remains in the previous scene, while the audio is already the anomalous sound of the next scene. Use sound to "invade" the current scene, creating a sense of creeping dread.
