# Comedy Humor · Director Narrative Techniques Skill Pack

## Introduction

This skill pack provides a complete reference set of director narrative techniques for **comedy humor** stories, covering full-process guidance from macroscopic narrative planning to microscopic storyboard execution. It is applicable to any visual style.

## Core Concepts

-   **Expectation Subversion** — The essence of a laugh is "the audience expects one thing, but something else happens." The greater the discrepancy, the stronger the comedic effect.
-   **Rhythm is Comedy** — The same joke can be hilarious with the right rhythm, or awkward with the wrong rhythm. Timing is everything.
-   **Characters Before Gags** — The most sophisticated comedy isn't about clever jokes, but about the absurdity that naturally arises from character clashes.
-   **Laughter Through Tears** — Pure comedy can easily lead to fatigue. Bury genuine emotions within the absurdity, allowing laughter to suddenly turn into profound emotion.

# Narrative Techniques · Comedy & Humor · Reference Guide

---

## I. Thematic Intent and the Comedy Core

### Comedy Narrative Essentials

- **Expectation violation is the engine of humor** — The underlying mechanism for all jokes is "expectation violation": the audience expects the character to go left, but they go right; they expect them to cry, but they hiccup instead. The director's job is to establish an expectation first, then break it precisely.
- **Character collision creates humor** — The highest form of comedy doesn't rely on gags, but on the chemistry of character personalities. Placing a deadpan, serious person alongside someone eccentric makes for naturally funny dialogue. The greater the personality contrast, the stronger the collision.
- **Absurdity must have internal logic** — When an absurd event occurs, the characters must "take it seriously." The more serious the characters are, the more absurd the audience perceives the situation. If the characters themselves realize it's absurd, the humor evaporates.
- **Recommended audience takeaways** — Hearty laughter / knowing smiles / tears of laughter / absurd relief. Avoid directions like "fear," "heaviness," or "desolation," which do not align with the comedic tone.
- **Tears of laughter is the highest realm** — Pure slapstick is forgotten as soon as the laughter ends, but revealing a character's genuine vulnerability or kindness in the midst of absurdity makes the audience laugh until their eyes water—this emotional contrast is the most powerful weapon in comedy.

---

## II. Narrative Structure and Comedic Timing

### Comedy Narrative Essentials

- **The Setup-to-Punchline basic unit** — Every joke consists of a "setup" and a "punchline." The setup establishes expectations (the more serious, the better), and the punchline breaks them (the more unexpected, the better). Neither can function without the other.
- **The Rule of Three** — The classic comedy rhythm: first pass establishes a pattern → second pass reinforces the pattern → third pass breaks the pattern. The audience thinks the third pass will be the same, but it isn't—that is the joke.
- **Progressive absurdity** — The level of absurdity should escalate: minor absurdity → moderate absurdity → major absurdity. Starting with extreme absurdity makes it hard for the audience to buy in; gradually increasing the level keeps the humor alive.
- **Joke intervals of no more than 60 seconds** — In a comedy narrative, the gap between two jokes should not exceed 60 seconds of narrative time. If more than 60 seconds pass without a humorous output, the audience's "comedic expectation" cools down.
- **Serious segments are the best setups** — Serious or moving segments interspersed in comedy don't interrupt the humor; they serve as the longest possible setup for the next joke. The more touching the moment, the greater the contrast for the following punchline.
- **The misunderstanding chain** — The classic engine of comedic narrative is the "misunderstanding": A misunderstands B → B misunderstands A's misunderstanding → C doesn't know that AB are both misunderstanding → the misunderstanding snowballs → finally, the truth is revealed, and all absurdity turns into laughter.

---

## III. Scene-Specific Humor Design

### Comedy Narrative Essentials

- **Describe humor through contrast** — Don't say "he looks funny," say "he is performing CPR on a goldfish with a solemn expression." Concrete descriptions of contrast are inherently humorous.
- **Typical comedic segments and design** —

| Segment Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Normal Opening | Calm + Routine | Everything is normal, but the character has a minor quirk/obsession | Normal daily background music (builds tension for the contrast) |
| Minor Loss of Control | Surprise + Humorous | A small accident triggers a chain reaction | Keep music normal (contrast strengthens humor) |
| Escalating Misunderstanding | Embarrassment + Absurdity | The more they explain, the worse it gets; the more they fix it, the more it crumbles | Light, accelerating tempo to hint at loss of control |
| Major Loss of Control | Complete Chaos | All characters fall into their own respective absurdities | Full, upbeat music or sudden total silence |
| Genuine Emotion | Tears of Laughter | Sudden reveal of true emotion amidst the absurdity | Music stops abruptly; keep only ambient sound |
| Return/Final Punchline | Relief + Knowing | A small final joke after everything settles | Light, concluding melody |

- **Escalating embarrassment is a core narrative for comedy** — The progression of awkwardness:
  - **Level 1**: The character is embarrassed themselves (slips of the tongue, tripping).
  - **Level 2**: Others witness the character's embarrassment (social death).
  - **Level 3**: The character tries to fix it but makes it worse (digging a deeper hole).
  - **Level 4**: Total loss of control, everyone is embarrassed (collective social death).
- **Space is an accomplice to comedy** — Use spatial elements to create humor: too many people in a small room, absurd events in solemn occasions, or mismatched people in completely mismatched locations.
- **Camera intent should write the "source of humor" rather than "what to shoot"** — "Use a wide shot to let the audience see the ten oblivious people standing behind him" is better than "use a wide shot to film the scene." Once the source of humor is clear, the storyboard naturally follows.

---

## IV. Sound and Music Direction

### Comedy Narrative Essentials

- **Contrasting music is a tool for humor** — Absurd visuals paired with serious music (classical/solemn marches) are funnier than pairing them with upbeat music. The audio-visual contrast is a form of "expectation violation" itself.
- **Sudden silence = the biggest joke** — When lively music cuts out abruptly while a character performs an extremely absurd action, the absurdity in the silence has more impact than it would with music.
- **Avoid "funny music"** — Do not use music that "sounds inherently funny" (slide whistles, comical percussion effects). Let the visuals generate the humor; keep the music neutral or even serious to allow the contrast to create the humor.
- **Comedic functions of ambient sound** — Specific ambient sounds can be part of the joke: a cell phone ringing during a solemn speech, a stomach growling in a quiet exam room.
- **Music emotion model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Normal Setup | Serious music (contrasts with the upcoming absurdity) | Medium |
| Minor Loss of Control | Music remains unchanged (pretending everything is fine) | Medium |
| Major Loss of Control | Music accelerates or cuts to total silence | Extreme |
| Joke Release | Brief silence followed by recovery | Low → Medium |
| Genuine Emotion | Music stops abruptly, pure ambient sound | None |
| Return to Routine | Light, concluding melody | Low-Medium |

- **"Metronome" humor** — Recurring jokes (the same mistake happening repeatedly) can use background sound with a fixed tempo to reinforce the expectation of "here we go again," maximizing the joke when the rhythm is broken the third time.

---

## V. Composition and Shot Size Narrative

### Comedy Narrative Essentials

- **Three core compositions and their narrative functions** —
  - **Reaction shot composition** — Place the gag-maker in the foreground/off-screen and the reactor in the center of the frame. Often, the bystander's expression is funnier than the joke itself.
  - **Wide shot exposure composition** — The wide shot reveals information the character is unaware of (there's someone behind them / their pants are torn / there's something absurd in the background), giving the audience an edge over the characters.
  - **Juxtaposition composition** — Two mismatched elements in one frame: a man in a suit and a chicken, a solemn ceremony and a chaotic background; juxtaposition is inherently humorous.
- **"Spotlight" effect of center composition** — Center composition in comedy creates the "the whole world is watching you make a fool of yourself" vibe, capturing the character's most awkward moments.
- **Visual information gap** — The core of comedic composition is "the audience sees what the character doesn't": on the left side of the screen, a character is giving a serious speech; on the right, someone is holding a banner with a typo.
- **Shot size equals comedic rhythm** —
  - **Long Shot/Wide Shot** — Exposes global absurdity, reveals information the character doesn't know.
  - **Medium Shot** — Standard narrative, dialogue interaction, character collision.
  - **Close-up** — The core shot for reaction shots, capturing "Huh?" or "What?" expressions.
  - **Extreme Close-up** — Magnifies awkward details: twitching lips, frozen smiles, beads of sweat on the forehead.
- **"Frozen joke" extreme close-up** — Extreme close-ups function as a "freeze-frame magnification" in comedy: a hand in the wrong place, an incorrect name tag, a completely mismatched expression. Holding for 1–2s is sufficient.

---

## VI. Camera Movement and Pacing

### Comedy Narrative Essentials

- **Calm observation is the best perspective for comedy** — More than 60% of shots should be static, recording the absurdity with the demeanor of a "calm observer." The calmer the camera, the more stark the contrast with the absurdity in the frame.
- **Snap-zoom (Zolly/Fast zoom) = Reaction magnification** — When a character hears/sees something absurd, a fast zoom into their face amplifies the "petrified/shocked/incredulous" reaction. This is the most frequent camera move in comedy.
- **Whip pan = Information revelation** — A rapid whip pan from Character A to Character B or an absurd object; the speed itself carries the humor of "Wait! What is that?!"
- **Stable vs. Chaotic** — Use stable shots for setup segments (everything is normal); for loss-of-control segments, the camera can be slightly unstable or even handheld (following the chaos). The transition from stable to unstable itself marks the "start of the fun."
- **Camera movement is rhythm** — Static = calm observation/setup; Snap-zoom = reaction magnification; Whip pan = information revelation; Handheld tracking = following chaos; Slow push = discovering absurd details.
- **Comedic function of slow motion** — In comedy, slow motion is used for "the moment of disaster": things flying, people slipping, cakes hitting faces—slow motion gives the audience enough time to "foresee the disaster" but be powerless to stop it; the joke accumulates in the anticipation.
- **Don't move the camera frequently** — Comedy does not need flashy camera moves. The simpler and more plain the camera, the more the character's absurdity stands out. Excessive movement distracts from the punchline.

# Storyboard Narrative Techniques · Comedy · Technique Reference

---

## 1. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the core tool for a director to translate a script into cinematic language. Table fields should be set by the director based on project needs (e.g., shot number, shot size, camera movement, duration, characters, events, dialogue, lighting, mood, transitions). The following is a reference for comedy-specific techniques.

---

## 2. Shot Size Selection

- **Reaction shots are the comedy ace** — A "reaction shot" (the observer's expression/the protagonist's petrification) must immediately follow a punchline, captured in a close-up. A punchline without a reaction shot is a dud.
- **Wide shots expose information** — The core function of a wide shot in comedy is to "let the audience see the absurdity that the character cannot": someone behind them, anomalies in the background, or an impending disaster. Wide shot = God's-eye view for the audience.
- **Medium shots carry dialogue** — The primary shot for character interaction/collision, maintaining enough visual data for the audience to read body language and expressions.
- **Close-ups freeze awkwardness** — Close-ups magnify awkward details in comedy: misspelled words, mislaid items, or frozen smiles. Hold briefly (1-2s) but with high impact.
- **Establishing shots should contain seeds of humor** — Comedy establishing shots can hide an "off" detail within a seemingly normal scene (someone doing something strange in the background, a typo on a sign) to set the stage for later laughs.

---

## 3. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Static shots are primary** — Over 60% of shots should be static; a calm, observational perspective makes the absurdity stand out more.
- **Snap-zoom = reaction amplification** — After a punchline, quickly zoom into the reactor's face to magnify shock, petrification, or disbelief. This is the most frequently used move in comedy storyboards.
- **Whip-pan = revealing absurdity** — Rapidly whip from A to B; the speed itself conveys the "What?!" of surprise.
- **Uncontrolled tracking** — When characters are in chaos (chasing, fleeing, fumbling), use handheld tracking shots; the shakiness conveys that "everything is out of control."
- **Slow push = discovering details** — Slowly push into an absurd detail, giving the audience a "Wait, let me see that... Ha!" moment of discovery.
- **Movement should not steal the show** — The principle of comedy camera movement is "do not upstage the character." Simpler is better; flashy moves distract from the punchline.

---

## 4. Duration Control

- **Allow enough time for setup** — Setup shots should be 3-4s to build audience expectation. If the setup is insufficient, the payoff won't land.
- **Precise punchline timing** — Punchline shots should be 1.5-2s—crisp and clean. The impact of a joke lies in its "suddenness"; dragging it out kills the humor.
- **Reaction shots 2-3s** — Give observers/protagonists enough "freeze" time for the audience to digest the joke.
- **Empty space for laughs** — Leave 1-2s of "silence" after a punchline (character silence/stagnant air) to give the audience time to laugh. If you push the narrative immediately, the laughter will be cut off.
- **The Golden 6-Second Rule** — Comedy pacing requires most individual shots to be no longer than 4s (faster than other genres). Only setup and emotional moments should reach 5-6s.
- **The Rule of Three duration progression** — When repeating a joke three times: 1st time = normal duration → 2nd time = slightly shorter (audience knows expectation) → 3rd time = shortest or longest (breaking the pattern).

---

## 5. Punchline Storyboard Design

- **Setup → Payoff shot structure** — Setup shot (medium/close-up, character doing something seriously) → Payoff shot (wide shot revealing absurdity/close-up magnifying the error/medium shot showing petrification). Two shots make one punchline unit.
- **Visual humor needs no dialogue** — Pure visual gags (tripping over something, absurd background events, mispositioned props) should be presented in wide shots or medium shots with camera movement, independent of dialogue.
- **Information gap gags** — Create expectation using information the audience knows but the character doesn't. Storyboard: Wide shot gives audience the info → Cut back to medium/close-up of the character (oblivious) → Disaster happens.
- **Chain reaction gags** — A small mistake triggers a domino effect. Use rapid hard cuts to connect every "collapse" phase (2s/shot), with the pace accelerating.
- **Don't break a comedy scene if it can be a single shot** — A long take where a character gradually descends from normal into chaos is more "irredeemably" funny than fragmented cuts.

---

## 6. Multi-Character Staging

- **Ensemble reaction chains** — After a punchline, cut through the reactions of every bystander in sequence: A stunned → B snickering → C pretends not to see → D feels more awkward than the protagonist. The chain itself extends the joke.
- **Use shot-reverse-shot for collisions** — Use alternating cuts for characters with clashing personalities; increase the tempo (1.5-2s per line). The sense of collision is externalized through editing speed.
- **Bystanders are the best gag amplifiers** — Always keep a "bystander position" in the storyboard: a passerby, an unrelated character, a pet—their reactions help the audience "confirm" that this situation is indeed absurd.
- **Use wide shots for group chaos** — When multiple people descend into chaos, use a single wide shot to present it, letting the audience see all the absurdity at once. The massive information density of a wide shot is inherently comedic.

---

## 7. Dialogue and Rhythm

- **Dialogue beat determines the punchline** — Comedy dialogue relies on timing: normal pace for setup → slight pause before punchline (0.5s) → punchline delivered crisply in one sentence. The pause is the "fuse" for the joke.
- **Deliver punchlines quickly** — Retorts/comebacks must land within 0.5s of the previous line finishing; fast counter-attacks are satisfying. In storyboards, give these no extra setup—just hard cut to close-up.
- **The humor of silence** — After something absurd happens, characters remaining silent for 2-3s is funnier than any dialogue. The characters' micro-expressions during this silence (pretending to be fine/petrified/slowly turning heads) are the core of the joke.
- **The power of repetition** — Repeat the same line/action three times; the third time should be a variation (messing it up/doing it wrong/laughing at oneself). In the storyboard, use the same frame and composition for the first two, then break it on the third.
- **Voiceover as a satirical tool** — In comedy, a voiceover can act as a "sarcastic commentator," describing absurd scenes in a deadpan tone. VO works best over wide or panoramic shots.

---

## 8. Transition Design

- **Absurdist jump cut** — Hard cut from one extreme directly to another (Character says "Absolutely impossible" → next shot shows them doing exactly that); the hard cut itself is the punchline.
- **Deadpan transitions** — Use formal/solemn transition methods (fade-in/fade-out, "Three Hours Later" title cards) to transition between absurd content, creating ironic contrast.
- **Rapid hard cuts within the same scene** — Comedy editing pacing within a scene should be snappy, prioritizing hard cuts without dragging.
- **Contrast montage** — Alternate between the character's "ideal plan" as narrated and the actual "disastrous execution." The contrast between ideal vs. reality creates sustained humor.
- **Breaking the fourth wall** — The moment a character suddenly looks into the camera/talks to the audience, use a head-on close-up + sudden freeze to create a sense of conspiracy ("Did you see that?").
