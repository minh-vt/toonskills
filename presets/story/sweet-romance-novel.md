# Sweet Romance: Director's Narrative Techniques Skill Pack

## Introduction

This skill pack provides a complete set of director's narrative technique references for the **Sweet Romance** genre, covering the entire process from macro-narrative planning to micro-storyboard execution. It is applicable to any visual style.

## Core Concepts

- **Subtlety and Restraint** — Emotional expression relies on negative space and subtle reactions rather than extensive dialogue.
- **Small Details, Big Impact** — Use details to move the audience: a glance, a moment of hesitation, a corner of a garment ruffled by the wind.
- **Push-Pull Rhythm** — Approach → Withdraw → Approach again → Misunderstanding → Separation → Reunion; each cycle becomes deeper and sweeter.
- **The Power of Silence** — In key emotional moments, silence is more powerful than a musical score.

# Narrative Technique · Sweet Romance · Technique Reference

---

## I. Thematic Conceptualization and Emotional Core

### Key Points for Sweet Romance Narrative

- **Subtlety takes precedence** — Emotional expression should not rely on expositional dialogue, but rather on "space left blank" (ma) and subtle reactions. Themes should lean towards restraint and understatement, avoiding blatant melodrama.
- **Restraint in "sweetness"** — "Almost touching" is more effective than "clinging together." The emotional arc should be designed with a "push-pull" rhythm where the affection is held back; the sweetness comes from the audience filling in the gaps themselves.
- **Small details, big impact** — Do not chase emotional impact through grand spectacles; move the audience with details: a glance, a hesitant pause, a corner of clothing ruffled by the wind.
- **Recommended departure emotions** — Heart-aching / lingering longing / heart-fluttering / healing. Avoid directions like "gratification" or "high-octane action," which do not match the tone of Sweet Romance.
- **Warmth in coldness, intimacy in distance** — Sweet romance does not mean saccharine. The overall tone can be cold or detached, but warmth should be released at critical nodes; contrast provides the greatest sweetness.

---

## II. Narrative Structure and Pacing Planning

### Key Points for Sweet Romance Narrative

- **Slowness is a foundational skill** — Sweet romance carries high-density emotional information (glances, micro-expressions, physical proximity), requiring time for the audience to "feel." The overall pace should be slow, but this does not mean dragging—every segment must provide emotional growth.
- **Gentle emotional curves** — Avoid "flat → sudden explosion." Use progressive emotional accumulation, where each segment is one level more intense than the last.
- **Use action, not dialogue, for turning points** — Handle critical turning points through actions (a sudden change in gaze, change in physical proximity, silence, passing an object) rather than relying on dialogue for explanation.
- **Use emotional buffers between segments** — Transitions require emotional breathing room; do not cut abruptly. Use ambient wide shots, solitary segments, or fragments of daily life to provide "breathing space."
- **The "speed" of climax is not fast editing** — It is high emotional density. You can use tighter framing transitions (Full shot → Medium close-up → Close-up → Extreme close-up) to create a heart-pounding sensation, rather than shortening shot duration.
- **Push-pull rhythm model** — The core engine of sweet romance is "push and pull": approach → retreat → approach again → misunderstanding → separation → reunion. Each cycle of push and pull should be deeper, more painful, and sweeter than the last.

---

## III. Scene-by-Scene Mood Design

### Key Points for Sweet Romance Narrative

- **Use concrete words for emotional goals** — Instead of "happy," use "the corner of the mouth that can't be suppressed after a secret thrill." Concrete emotional descriptions better guide frame selection and performance details.
- **Typical emotional segments and design** —

| Segment Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| First Meeting / Reveal | Stunning + Curious | "Discovering" the other from an observer's perspective, starting from far to near | Minimalist, use only ambient sound to create "breathlessness" |
| Daily Infatuation | Undercurrent + Restraint | Peeking, hesitation, intentionally keeping distance | Soft instrumental, low volume, background |
| Misunderstanding / Separation | Heartache + Forbearance | No explanations, turning away, crying in solitude | Melancholic solo or pure ambient sound |
| Confession / Reconciliation | Relief + Thrill | Speaking after silence, gaze before words | Warm instrumental gradually fading in |
| Escalation / Ambiguity | Nervous + Sweet | Decreasing physical distance, light touches, audible breathing | Light rhythmic elements, hinting at heartbeats |
| High Sweetness / Marriage | Happiness + Solemnity | Ritualistic, solemn eye contact, vows | Rich instrumental, solemn but gentle |

- **"Distance" is a core narrative tool** — Use physical distance between characters to map the progress of the relationship:
  - **Early Stage**: Wide/Half-body shot, large physical distance, polite speech.
  - **Mid Stage**: Medium shot, distance shortened but obstructed (objects/crowds/hesitation).
  - **Late Stage**: Close-up/Extreme close-up, zero distance, psychological defenses dropped.
- **Spatial elements as emotional metaphors** — Use scene elements to convey emotions, reducing reliance on dialogue. For example: A blurred silhouette behind a curtain = estrangement; opening a door to see a courtyard of flowers = relief; sitting alone in the rain = loneliness.
- **Write the "why," not the "how" for shot intent** — "Use a close-up to let the audience see the hesitation in her eyes" is superior to "use a close-up to shoot her face." When the intent is clear, the storyboard naturally dictates the right frame and angle.

---

## IV. Sound and Music Direction

### Key Points for Sweet Romance Narrative

- **Silence is more powerful than score** — For key emotional moments (eye contact, falling tears, turning away), prioritize removing the score and leaving only ambient sound. The "sweetness" of the genre is often imagined by the audience after a moment of silence.
- **Score follows the emotional arc of segments** — Do not score scene by scene; divide the narrative into segments and set a musical mood for each. Use ambient sound changes to transition between scenes within a segment, avoiding frequent track changes.
- **Avoid wall-to-wall music** — It is recommended that score coverage does not exceed 60% of the total film. The "silence" in blank segments creates a breathing rhythm against the scored segments.
- **Ambient sound is half the atmosphere** — Note 1-2 core ambient sounds for every scene to assist subsequent sound design. The richer the ambient layers, the more immersive the scene.
- **Musical emotional progression model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Stable / Daily | Soft instrumental background | Low |
| Undercurrent / Brewing | Solo instrument, very low volume | Medium-Low |
| Emotional Burst | Gradually full instrumental or sudden silence | Medium-High |
| Fate Turning Point | Intense instrumental or total silence | Extreme |
| Warming / Healing | Warm instrumental easing in | Medium |

- **The "Heartbeat" of sweet romance** — During segments of escalating ambiguity, use light rhythmic percussion (hand drums, woodblocks, plucked strings) to imply a racing heart, which is more sophisticated than using sweet melodies alone.

---

## V. Composition and Framing Narrative

### Key Points for Sweet Romance Narrative

- **Narrative function of three core compositions** —
  - **Heavy negative space** — Loneliness/atmosphere/poetic space, conveying the character's psychological isolation or emotional gaps.
  - **Framed composition** — Silhouettes behind curtains/door frames/window lattices/screens, creating a "peeking" perspective of infatuation and a sense of barrier.
  - **Rule of thirds** — Dialogue/daily life/interaction between two, stable and balanced, suitable for daily sweet segments.
- **Limited use of center composition** — Reserve center composition for formal reveals and ritualistic scenes (e.g., weddings, formal confessions). Do not use for daily life, or it loses its impact.
- **Spatial depth as narrative** — Foreground obstruction (curtain/flower branches/smoke) + Mid-ground subject + Background environment. More layers mean stronger feelings of estrangement; fewer layers mean more intimacy.
- **Vertical vs. Horizontal composition** — Vertical for solo close-ups/reveals (emphasizing loneliness and posture); horizontal for duos/scenes (emphasizing relationship and shared space).
- **Sweet romance framing progression** — Within the same scene, framing should progress with emotional escalation: Half-body → Medium close-up → Close-up → Extreme close-up. Do not go straight to a close-up; leave room for emotional ascent.
- **Reasons for extreme close-ups** — Extreme close-ups (eyes/lips/hands) are "emotional nukes"; 2-3 times per episode is sufficient. Overuse leads to audience fatigue.
- **Wide shots are not just transitions** — Wide shots have inherent narrative value (loneliness, spatial pressure, seasonal atmosphere). Give wide shots sufficient duration (4-6s); do not cut away too quickly.

---

## VI. Camera Movement and Pacing

### Key Points for Sweet Romance Narrative

- **Static shots are primary** — More than 60% of shots should be static, letting the details and emotions speak for themselves.
- **Slow push-in = Approach/Heart-flutter** — Psychological suggestion of "audience approaching the character," suitable for heart-flutters, discovery, or voyeurism.
- **Slow pull-out = Detachment/Loneliness** — Psychological suggestion of "audience receding," suitable for departure, loneliness, or revealing the full picture.
- **Fast-cutting is incompatible** — Fast editing is incompatible with the temperament of sweet romance. Even in climax segments, use frame progression rather than fast cuts to build rhythm.
- **Pans and tracking shots** — Slow pans are suitable for showing the full scope of a scene or following a character walking; tracking shots are suitable for rituals or walking scenes. Speed should always be restrained.
- **Camera movement is emotion** — Movement is not a technical choice; it is an emotional one. Static = stable/suppressed; slow push = approach/thrill; slow pull = detachment/loneliness; slow pan = display/solemnity.
- **Sweet romance "heartbeat movement"** — In segments of rising ambiguity, use micro-slow push-ins combined with frame progression (Half-body → Medium close-up → Close-up) to simulate the physiological feeling of "narrowing attention" when one's heart races.

# Storyboard Narrative Techniques · Sweet Romance · Technical Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the core tool for a director to translate a script into visual language. Fields in the table should be customized by the director according to project needs (e.g., Shot Number, Shot Size, Camera Movement, Duration, Characters, Events, Dialogue, Lighting, Emotion, Transitions, etc.). The following provides technical references specific to the sweet romance narrative genre.

---

## II. Shot Size Selection

- **Progression in Sweet Scenes** — Within the same scene, shot sizes should progress as emotions intensify: Medium Shot → Close-up → Tight Close-up. Avoid starting with a tight close-up; leave room for emotional build-up.
- **Long Shots are Not Mere Transitions** — Long shots have inherent narrative value (loneliness, spatial pressure, seasonal atmosphere). Give them sufficient duration (4-6s) and do not cut away prematurely.
- **Justify Tight Close-ups** — Tight close-ups (eyes/lips/hands) are "emotional nuclear bombs." Use them 2-3 times per episode at most; overuse leads to audience fatigue.
- **Streamline Establishing Shots** — Use a maximum of 1-2 shots to establish a scene; do not break it into more than three fragments. Typical approach: 1 Extreme Long/Long Shot for establishment + 1 Full Shot to introduce the subject, or a single Long Shot with a slow zoom-in to combine establishment and introduction. Avoid the redundant three-part formula of "environment shot → detail shot → character arrival."

---

## III. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Default to Static** — Over 60% of shots should use a static camera, allowing screen details and emotions to speak for themselves.
- **Slow Push-in = Emotional Intensification** — Creates the psychological implication of "the audience moving closer to the character." Suitable for infatuation, discovery, or spying.
- **Slow Pull-back = Emotional Detachment** — Creates the psychological implication of "the audience stepping back." Suitable for parting, loneliness, or revealing the bigger picture.
- **Movement is Emotion** — Camera movement is an emotional choice, not just a technical one. Static = Stability/Suppression; Slow Push-in = Proximity/Infatuation; Slow Pull-back = Detachment/Loneliness; Slow Pan = Display/Solemnity.
- **Sweet Romance "Heartbeat Movement"** — During scenes of growing intimacy, use subtle slow push-ins accompanied by progressing shot sizes (Medium → Close-up → Tight Close-up) to simulate the physiological sensation of "narrowing focus" during a racing heartbeat.

---

## IV. Duration Control

- **Close-up/Expression Shots** — 2-3s, focusing on subtle micro-expression changes.
- **Dialogue Close-ups** — 3-4s, for stable delivery of lines.
- **Full Body Reveal** — 3-5s, to show the full figure.
- **Long Shots/Empty Shots** — 4-6s, for atmospheric rendering.
- **No Single Shot Over 6s** — Audience attention wanes after 6s unless sustained by camera movement or dynamic elements.
- **The Golden 6-Second Rule** — If no new information (dialogue/action/subject change) occurs within 6s of a shot without dialogue, audience focus breaks. This is especially true for establishing/transition shots—merge or compress them rather than letting them drag.

---

## V. Shot Merging Strategy (Reducing AI "Stiffness")

- **One Shot, One Idea** — If a camera movement (e.g., a slow push-in from Long to Full shot) can establish a scene and introduce the subject simultaneously, do not split it into "empty shot for establishment → cut to subject entering frame."
- **Merge Consecutive Similar Info** — Shots describing different parts of the same space (gate → vines → burnt room) should be merged into one shot, covering multiple layers of spatial information within the frame.
- **Narrative Density First** — Every shot must advance the narrative or emotion. Purely decorative shots (used only to show environmental details) should be merged into narrative-driven shots.
- **Director’s Perspective Check** — After writing the storyboard, ask yourself: if a live-action director would combine these 2-3 adjacent shots into one, then they are likely too fragmented and should be merged.

---

## VI. One-Shot Sequences (Long Take Merging)

- **Conditions for Use** — When adjacent shots involve continuous movement, minor scene changes (displacement within the same scene), or gradual angle shifts, prioritize a one-shot sequence over fragmentation for smoother visual flow.
- **Typical Scenarios** — Characters walking through a space, following action from point A to point B, circling a character to reveal the environment, or a slow push from an establishing shot to a character close-up.
- **Annotation Method** — Clearly write the full path in the Camera Movement field (e.g., "One-shot: Slow push from Long Shot → track to interior → land on Full Shot"), and describe the start and end points in the Visual Description.
- **Extended Duration** — Due to the continuous flow of information, these may exceed the 6s limit, but should stay under 12s.
- **Generation Risk** — One-shot sequences require high consistency in image generation, increasing the difficulty of the prompt. Only use when the narrative benefits significantly outweigh fragmentation; limit to 2-3 instances per episode.

---

## VII. Interaction Design

- **Limit Actions to Two per Shot** — "Bowing to pick a flower + smiling" is fine; "Bowing to pick a flower + smiling + turning + raising a hand" will cause visual collapse.
- **Use Suggestion for Intimacy** — Fingers almost touching, sleeves brushing, gaze following and then looking away. Do not write "hug" or "kiss" (major dual-character interactions) in the storyboard; break them down into suggestive detail shots.

---

## VIII. Dialogue and "Negative Space"

- **Longer Durations for Sparse Dialogue** — Emotional shots without dialogue often need more time than those with lines. 3 seconds of silence can hold more tension than a line of dialogue.
- **One Line, One Shot** — Avoid stuffing multiple lines of dialogue into a single shot; cut to the speaker when the dialogue switches.
- **Use Long/Empty Shots for Voiceover** — Internal monologues often look unnatural in close-ups if the lips aren't moving; pair them with long or empty atmospheric shots for a more natural feel.

---

## IX. Transition Design

- **Default to Hard Cuts** — Use hard cuts for shots within the same scene for a clean, crisp feel.
- **Use Empty Shots for Scene Transitions** — Insert an empty atmospheric shot (2-3s) between different scenes as an emotional buffer.
- **Use Dissolves/Fades for Paragraph/Segment Shifts** — Use soft transitions for major shifts in emotional tone between long segments to prevent audience distraction.
