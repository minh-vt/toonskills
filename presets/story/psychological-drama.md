# Psychological Game · Director's Narrative Techniques Skill Pack

## Introduction

This skill pack provides a complete set of directorial narrative technique references for the **Psychological Game** genre, covering the entire process from macro narrative planning to micro storyboard execution. It is applicable to any visual style.

## Core Concepts

- **Calm Surface, Turbulent Undercurrents** — The core charm of a psychological game lies in the fact that "what is said is different from what is thought." Every sentence has an subtext, and every smile could be a trap.
- **Information is the Weapon** — Whoever controls information holds the initiative. Narrative tension arises from information asymmetry, concealment, and revelation.
- **Multi-layered Games** — The highest level of psychological maneuvering is not "A deceiving B," but a "cognitive Matryoshka" where A knows B is lying, B knows A knows, and A knows B knows A knows.
- **Shades of Grey in Human Nature** — There are no absolute heroes or villains in psychological game narratives; every character has their own logic and hidden struggles.

# Narrative Techniques · Psychological Games · Reference Guide

---

## I. Thematic Conception and Emotional Core

### Key Points for Psychological Game Narratives

- **Surface Calm, Undercurrents Flowing** — The core allure of psychological games lies in "duality." Every seemingly ordinary line of dialogue has hidden subtext; every smile may be a carefully calculated strategy. The thematic intent should revolve around an "invisible war of offense and defense between individuals."
- **Information as a Weapon** — The underlying engine of psychological game narratives is "information asymmetry." Who knows what, who doesn't know, and who pretends not to know—the concealment, speculation, and revelation of information form the core of narrative tension.
- **Multi-layered Cognitive Games** — The highest form of psychological gaming is not simple deception, but "cognitive nesting": A thinks they are fooling B → B actually knew all along → A knows B knows but pretends not to know. The more layers, the greater the intellectual pleasure for the audience.
- **Recommended Audience Takeaways** — Brain-burning / Chills down the spine / Sudden realization / Creepy. Avoid purely sensory directions like "gore" or "violence."
- **The Gray Area of Humanity** — There are no absolute heroes or villains in psychological narratives. Every character has their own logic, grievances, and bottom lines. The best games result in "the audience understanding the motives of every party, but not knowing which side to take."

---

## II. Narrative Structure and Pacing Planning

### Key Points for Psychological Game Narratives

- **Information Control is Fundamental** — The core task of psychological narrative is manipulating information flow. During the planning stage, define clearly: in each segment, what does the audience know, what does Character A know, and what does Character B know? The gap between these three is the source of tension.
- **Perspective Shifts to Create Cognitive Flips** — When the same event is presented from different character perspectives, the audience's understanding flips with each shift. Planning should mark "perspective shift points" and the cognitive updates brought by each.
- **Twists Require Foreshadowing** — Every twist must be preceded by sufficient clues. At the moment of a twist, the audience should feel "I should have seen that coming!" rather than "That came out of nowhere." A recommended ratio of foreshadowing to twists is 3:1 (three parts foreshadowing for one twist).
- **Tension Accumulation → Brief Release → Greater Tension** — The rhythm of a psychological game is a spiral: there is a moment of relaxation after each round, but the stakes of the next round are higher and the risks greater.
- **The "Exposure" Pacing Model** — The core rhythm of a psychological game is the gradual exposure of a character's "hidden cards": Disguise → Cracks appear → Opponent notices → Turning the tables → Deeper disguise → Greater exposure. Each round brings us closer to the truth than the last.
- **Audience Positioning Design** — Explicitly define if the audience has an "Omniscient Perspective" (knowing both sides' cards) or a "Follow-along Perspective" (only knowing one side's information). These positions create different tensions: Omniscient = Anxiety as you watch a character walk into a trap; Follow-along = Shock as you are deceived alongside the character.

---

## III. Scene-Specific Mood Design

### Key Points for Psychological Game Narratives

- **Use Game-Oriented Vocabulary for Emotional Goals** — Instead of "tense," say "speaking with a smile, but palms are sweating." Instead of "scared," say "discovering the other person knows something, but not knowing how much they know." Psychological emotions are always multi-layered and contradictory.
- **Typical Emotional Segments and Design** —

| Segment Type | Emotional Direction | Narrative Technique | Music Suggestion |
|---|---|---|---|
| Surface Peace | Courtesies + Undercurrents | Normal dialogue, but every line has subtext | Extremely light backing or no music; let the dialogue tension carry the scene |
| Testing/Jousting | Tension + Intellectual Pleasure | Hidden attacks/defenses in back-and-forth dialogue; double meanings | Low-frequency undercurrents, subtle acceleration of pace |
| Cracks Appear | Unease + Alertness | A detail is exposed; a fleeting change in the opponent's expression | Dissonant interval flashes, then return to calm |
| Showdown/Twist | Shock + Realization | Cards are revealed; all previous clues suddenly connect | Sudden silence or a sustained low note |
| Standoff | Suffocation + Equilibrium | Both parties know that the other knows, but neither makes the first move | Complete silence; only ambient sound and breathing |
| Final Reveal | Subversion + Sigh | The final truth is deeper than anyone expected | Minimalist instrumentation or lingering echoes |

- **"Dialogue as Battlefield" is a Core Narrative Tool** — In psychological games, dialogue is not for information exchange, but a weapon of offense and defense:
  - **Probing Questions** = Testing/Offense (Trying to extract information)
  - **Evasion/Changing Topic** = Defense (Trying to conceal information)
  - **Counter-questions** = Counter-attack (Pushing the pressure back onto the opponent)
  - **Silence** = The strongest card (Making the other party anxious and prone to exposure)
- **Space is a Power Field** — In psychological game scenes, whoever occupies the initiative in the space holds the upper hand: Sitting at opposite ends of a table = Balanced game; Standing and pacing = Exerting pressure; Turning back = Confidence/Contempt; Trapped in a corner = Disadvantage.
- **Micro-expressions are the Truth** — An advanced technique is "the mouth lies, but the body tells the truth." In narrative planning, each game segment should note the character's "true reactions" (micro-expressions, unconscious movements), which serve as clues for the audience.

## IV. Sound and Music Direction

### Key Points for Psychological Game Narratives

- **Silence is the Strongest Weapon** — During key standoff segments, prioritize removing the music, leaving only ambient sound and the characters' breathing/heartbeats. Dialogue amidst silence is more intense than any score.
- **Music Creates Cognitive Suggesion** — Music in psychological games isn't meant to set a mood, but to guide the audience’s cognitive judgment. Music during a character's entrance can imply "this person is suspicious"; calm dialogue paired with unsettling bass suggests "there is a trap."
- **Deceptive Sound** — Use the score to "deceive" the audience along with the narrative: use warm music to make the audience believe a character is a hero; the dissonance at the moment of the twist becomes a narrative tool for "ah, I see now."
- **Ambient Sound as Psychological Space** — Echo in a confined space = Trapped/nowhere to run; Dialogue in a noisy environment = Secretive/inconvenient to speak the truth; Empty, quiet space = Nowhere to hide/everything can be heard.
- **Music Emotion Progression Model** —

| Emotional Stage | Music Strategy | Coverage |
|---|---|---|
| Surface Peace | Extremely light backing or none | Very Low |
| Undercurrents | Low-frequency dissonance, increasing volume | Mid-Low |
| Escalating Joust | Rhythmic suggestions, ramping up tension | Medium |
| Showdown/Twist | Sudden silence or one powerful note followed by stillness | Extreme |
| Final Reveal | Minimalist instrumentation, lingering effect | Low |

- **"Heartbeat Exposure"** — Use the sound of a character's heartbeat during key game segments. An accelerated heartbeat implies the character is lying/tense/about to be exposed. Use sparingly; becoming a "lie detector" for the character reduces suspense.

---

## V. Composition and Shot Scale Narrative

### Key Points for Psychological Game Narratives

- **Narrative Functions of Three Core Compositions** —
  - **Symmetrical Composition** — Two people facing each other/opposing, conveying "evenly matched/standoff/balanced game."
  - **Triangular Composition** — A third party (observer/variable/referee) in a three-way game, breaking binary opposition and adding complexity.
  - **Imbalanced Composition** — Subject shifted to one side with negative space or threatening elements on the other, implying "balance broken/someone is in the shadows."
- **Shot Scale is Information Authority** — Close-up = Audience is permitted to see micro-expressions (mind-reading); Medium Shot = Audience sees only body language (guessing); Wide Shot = Audience sees only positional relationships (judging the layout). Use shot scale to control the audience's "mind-reading privilege."
- **Offense and Defense of Dialogue Shot Scales** — In dialogue, the shot scale should change with the status of the game: The party with the upper hand gets a closer shot (confident/composed); the disadvantaged party gets a close-up (tense/revealing micro-expressions); even matches get two-person medium shots.
- **Spatial Depth is Game Layout** — Use spatial depth to convey the game relationship: Foreground = Current speaker (surface); Mid-ground = Listener (observing/calculating); Background = Third party or hidden information.
- **The Mind-Reading Function of Extreme Close-ups** — Extreme close-ups (pupils/corners of the mouth/fingertips) are used to capture "cracks in a lie" or "a fleeting change in expression upon being discovered." These are clues for the audience and must be precise.
- **"Things That Shouldn't Be Seen"** — Advanced compositional technique: Let the audience "accidentally" see something the character is trying to hide (a shaking hand behind the back, a clenched fist under the table, a fleeting shift in gaze).

---

## VI. Camera Movement and Pacing

### Key Points for Psychological Game Narratives

- **Absolute Stasis for Standoff Segments** — Core standoff segments should remain completely still, as if "holding its breath." Over 60% of shots should be static. Any camera movement distracts from micro-expressions and dialogue subtext.
- **Slow Push = Approaching Truth/Exerting Pressure** — An extremely slow push suggests "the audience/character is nearing the truth" or "one party is exerting pressure on the other." The slower the speed, the greater the pressure.
- **Slow Pull = Revealing the Full Picture/Discovery** — Slowly zooming out from a partial view to reveal the full picture the character hasn't noticed (someone listening in the shadows, evidence behind them) is a classic "audience knows but character doesn't know" technique.
- **Subtle Drift** — In a static shot, the camera drifts slowly to one side, implying "the scales of the game are tipping." Whoever it drifts toward is losing the advantage.
- **Quick Cuts for Twist Moments** — The only time quick cuts are allowed is the "cards revealed" moment: Rapid shot-scale jumps (Wide → Close-up → Extreme Close-up) to simulate the psychological impact of "sudden realization."
- **Movement is Psychological State** — Static = Standoff/Balance; Slow Push = Nearing/Pressure; Slow Pull = Revealing/Yielding; Drift = Tipping scales; Quick Cut = Truth exposed.

# Storyboard Narrative Techniques · Psychological Games · Technical Reference

---

## 1. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the core tool for a director to translate a script into visual language. Fields in the table are set by the director based on project needs (Shot No., Shot Size, Camera Movement, Duration, Characters, Events, Dialogue, Lighting, Emotion, Transitions, etc.). The following provides technical references specific to the "Psychological Game" narrative genre.

---

## 2. Shot Size Selection

- **Close-ups are Mind-Reading Tools** — In psychological games, close-ups are used more frequently than in other genres. They are used to capture the subtle facial flaws where a character's "words do not match their heart": a momentary flick of the eyes, an imperceptible lift of the corner of the mouth, or the contraction of pupils.
- **Medium Shots are the Game Arena** — The primary shot size for dialogue-heavy game sequences is the medium/near shot, ensuring the audience sees both the character's facial expressions and body language. Pure close-ups lose body information; pure wide shots lose facial nuance.
- **Wide Shots Establish the Landscape** — Wide shots are used to establish the "landscape" of the game: who is with whom, where the third party is, and the power dynamic within the space.
- **Extreme Close-ups deliver Clues** — Extreme close-ups (a trembling fingertip/dilating pupil/a fleeting twitch of the mouth) are used to drop key clues to the audience: this person is lying, this person has discovered something, or this person is about to act.
- **Establishing Shots must hide information** — Establishing shots in game narratives do more than just set the scene; they should convey the attributes of the "game arena" immediately: enclosed vs. open, presence of a third party, and where the escape route lies.

---

## 3. Camera Movement Rhythm

- **Default to Static, Static equals Standoff** — Over 65% of shots should be static. In psychological games, a static shot conveys the tension of "two people squaring off; whoever moves first reveals their flaw."
- **Slow Zoom-in = Closing In/Pressuring** — A speed slower than a standard slow zoom implies "the truth is being approached" or "one party is exerting psychological pressure."
- **Slow Zoom-out = Revealing/Discovery** — Pulling back slowly from a character to reveal information they are unaware of (someone behind them/evidence on the table/another character's reaction).
- **Subtle Pan = Tilting Scales** — A very slow, subtle panning of the camera in a static frame suggests the momentum of the game is shifting.
- **Fast Cuts are for Reversals only** — Rapid jump-cuts between shot sizes (Medium → Close-up → Extreme Close-up) are permitted only at the moment of a reveal to simulate cognitive shock. Fast cuts are prohibited in all other segments.

---

## 4. Duration Control

- **Dialogue Shots 3-5s** — The pace of shot-reverse-shot dialogue in psychological games is slightly slower than daily conversation. The silence between sentences represents the "thinking time" as both sides calculate.
- **Silent Standoff 4-6s** — Staring/silent segments without dialogue need sufficient duration for the audience to "feel what is happening in the air."
- **Micro-expression Close-ups 2-3s** — The duration of close-ups on micro-expression flaws must be precise: too short and the audience misses it; too long and it becomes an intentional emphasis.
- **Moments of Reversal 1-2s** — Key reaction shots when the "hidden card" is flipped should be short and precise—fast enough to shock the audience before they can react.
- **Wide Shots/Landscape Establishment 3-4s** — Wide shots establishing the game layout don't need to be long; once the information is delivered, move on.
- **Golden Rule: Silence is longer than dialogue** — In psychological games, the silence between two sentences should be longer than the sentences themselves. Silence is the game played between "playing cards."

---

## 5. Shot Merging Strategy (Removing AI-feel)

- **Maintain Shot-Reverse-Shot for Dialogue** — Unlike other genres, psychological games require precise shot-reverse-shots to convey the rhythm of attack and defense. Do not force them into two-person shots. Each cut acts as the "ball" being passed between two people.
- **Complete a Game Round in One Sequence** — A complete "attack-defense round" (probing → response → re-probing → showdown) should be planned as a cohesive set of shots to maintain internal rhythmic consistency.
- **Extremely High Narrative Density** — In psychological games, every shot must carry information (dialogue/subtext/micro-expressions/body clues/spatial hints). No "pure atmosphere" shots are allowed.
- **Director's Logic Check** — After storyboarding, self-reflect: Does every shot have an "information layer"? What can the audience "read" in this shot? If they can read nothing, the shot should not exist.

---

## 6. Long Takes (Merging Shots)

- **Conditions for Use** — When characters move through a space, power dynamics shift with spatial position, or characters transition from one state of information to another.
- **Typical Scenes** — Character A stands and walks to the window (from equal to overlooking); characters circle a table (power display); walking from a conference room to a hallway (public to private).
- **Notation** — Specify the full path in the camera movement field (e.g., "Long take: Character A stands up → slow zoom follows to the window → pan reveals Character B's reaction at the seat → land on a two-person composition"). Describe the start and end frames.
- **Relaxed Duration** — Because information density is constantly updating in game long takes, you may exceed the 6s single-shot limit, but do not exceed 12s.
- **Spatial Shift is Psychological Shift** — Every spatial move by a character in a long take should correspond to a change in the psychological game state.

---

## 7. Character Interaction Design

- **Dialogue is a Clash** — Every line of dialogue in the storyboard should be annotated with a "subtext" or "true intention." The dialogue is the surface; the subtext is the game.
- **Micro-actions are Flaws** — In every game shot, design a subtle "true reaction" action for the character: touching an ear inadvertently = nervous; tapping fingers on the table = calculating; drinking water = stalling.
- **Whoever speaks first loses** — Silence is a weapon in a standoff. The storyboard should note "who owns the silence"—who is creating the silence (active pressure) vs. who is enduring the silence (passive defense).

---

## 8. Dialogue and Negative Space

- **Every line has dual meanings** — There is no "filler dialogue" in psychological games. Lines in the storyboard should be concise and polysemous, allowing the audience to realize on a second viewing: "So that’s what that meant."
- **Silence is more dangerous than dialogue** — The most dangerous moment in a game is not the argument, but when one side suddenly stops speaking. Silence means "I have already planned the next step."
- **Information quantity in responses** — The amount of information in a response must be precisely controlled: too little = suspicious (hiding something); too much = suspicious (covering up); just right = most suspicious (too perfect).

---

## 9. Transition Design

- **Default to Hard Cuts** — Use hard cuts between shots within the same scene to maintain a crisp and tense game rhythm.
- **Brief Blackout between Game Rounds** — Use a 0.3–0.5s short black screen as a "breath" between the end of one game round and the beginning of the next, signaling that a "new round has begun."
- **Hard Cuts for Perspective Shifts** — Use a hard cut when switching from Character A’s perspective to Character B’s to create the impact of a "cognitive reversal."
- **Fast Flashes for Flashbacks** — When a character suddenly recalls a key clue during the game, insert a very short flashback (0.5–1s) to simulate a "flash of insight."
- **Maintain tension during scene changes** — Avoid soft transitions between different scenes to keep the psychological game’s tension taut throughout. If a buffer is needed, use a short shot of a character alone (thinking/planning the next move) as an emotional transition.
