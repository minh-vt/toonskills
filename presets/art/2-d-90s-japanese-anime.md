# 90s Japanese Anime Style Guide

This style is specifically designed for "90s Japanese Anime Aesthetic" themes. All art prompts, specifications, and generated content are strictly limited to the following:

- **90s Anime Worldview**: Set against the backdrop of 1990s Japanese life, including schools, train stations, streets, cafes, and residential areas, capturing the authentic atmosphere of Japanese daily life.
- **Hand-Drawn Cel-Shaded Texture**: All characters, scenes, and props must adhere to the standards of 1990s hand-drawn animation, emphasizing line clarity, uniform coloring, and layered light and shadow.
- **Nostalgic Healing Aesthetic**: Building upon cel-shading, the style pursues a cinematic atmosphere with soft, warm tones and rich details, creating an overall warm, healing, and poignant nostalgic feeling.
- **Emotional Expression**: Suitable for depicting gentle, healing, and romantic Japanese love stories, emphasizing emotional expression rooted in Eastern aesthetics.

## Scope of Application

- AI art generation for 90s Japanese anime themes (characters, scenes, props, storyboards, etc.)
- Visual creations requiring an authentic hand-drawn texture, delicate linework, and a warm, healing atmosphere.

## Prohibited Content

- Modern Japanese anime styles, 3D rendering, and CGI animation
- Modern elements, modern architecture, and modern fashion
- High-saturation fluorescent colors or neon colors
- Excessive shadows, dark/violent themes, or adult content

## Style Experience

Under this style, you will experience:

- Characters with refined features and gentle or clear temperaments, featuring 90s Japanese anime-style clothing, makeup, and hairstyles.
- Scenes with rich spatial depth, clear and fluid lines, and an atmosphere that is both realistic and poetic.
- Props, daily objects, and environmental details designed with Japanese aesthetics, featuring fine craftsmanship and delicate textures.
- Storyboards and visuals based on hand-drawn cel-shading, emphasizing narrative and emotional expression.

---

This style is only applicable to projects related to "90s Japanese Anime." If other styles are required (such as modern anime, CG, illustration, etc.), please do not use this set of prompts.

## I. Layering Principles

1. **Unchanged Facial Features** — The facial features must remain identical to the base model after layering; facial shifts are prohibited.
2. **Unchanged Pose** — Maintain the natural standing pose of the base model; any changes to pose, motion, or posture are prohibited.
3. **Layer-by-Layer Control** — Each layer must be described independently to facilitate layer-specific replacement (e.g., changing clothing without changing makeup).
4. **Unified Style** — All clothing and makeup elements must adhere to the 90s retro hand-drawn flat-color aesthetic system, without resorting to templated replication.
5. **Preservation of Character Differentiation** — Clothing should vary based on age, status, personality, and occasion to avoid "one-style-fits-all" designs.
6. **No Reduction in Quality** — The hand-drawn texture after layering must be equal to or better than the base model.
7. **Strictly Apparel-Focused** — Only overlay makeup, hairstyles, clothing, footwear, and accessories; the introduction of props, scenes, environments, or actions is prohibited.

# Anime Style Constraints · 90s Japanese Animation · Technical Reference

---

## I. Color Palette and Visual Tone

- **Base Tone** — The film uses warm yellow (C1), cherry blossom pink (C3), and sky blue (C6) as base colors. The overall color temperature is warm (4800-5200K) with low-to-medium saturation (50-70%), evoking the warm, nostalgic feeling of 90s hand-drawn animation. Maintain a soft warm base tone throughout, using cool colors (light blue C6, pale purple C7) as accents to add depth.
- **Cool/Warm Narrative Contrast** — Cool colors (light blue C6, pale purple C7) serve as visual signals for narrative shifts, used as local accents during nighttime or emotional transitions. Switches between cool and warm should be synchronized with the story arc, rather than mixed arbitrarily.
- **Palette-First Principle** — Sequence planning requires binding emotional scenes (daily life/heart-fluttering/farewell/reunion, etc.) to a specific palette and lighting scheme before production to avoid "the plot being correct but the emotional color being wrong."
- **Forbidden Color Gamut** — High-saturation fluorescent colors, neon colors, and modern CG rendering color systems are incompatible with this style.

---

## II. Lighting Scheme System

- **Lighting as Narrative** — 6 lighting schemes correspond to different emotional segments. During the director's planning stage, determine the lighting tone at the sequence level rather than specifying it shot-by-shot.
- **Light Angle** — Default to 30-45° oblique side lighting to simulate natural light effects.

| Emotional Segment | Lighting Direction | Color Tendency | Application Phase |
|---|---|---|---|
| Daily Warmth | A·Soft Diffusion | Warm yellow base + uniform soft light | Throughout |
| Heart-Fluttering Moment | B·Warm Side Light | Cherry blossom pink + warm orange accents | Rising tension |
| Office/School | C·Top Light + Ambient Light | Neutral warm + soft cool white | Work/Study |
| Sunset Romance | D·Warm Backlight | Amber warm dominant + cool blue background | Sweet moments |
| Night/Moonlight | E·Cool Moonlight | Light blue dominant + warm accents | Nighttime |
| Memory/Flashback | F·Warm Soft Focus | Warm yellow dominant + fogging effect | Memories |

- **Distribution of Cool and Warm Light** — Warm tones can be used throughout the film and adjusted flexibly based on scene needs; cool tones (light blue dominant) are reserved for nighttime or after emotional turning points.
- **Atmospheric Mapping** — The atmosphere of every scene should map to one of the directions in the lighting schemes (A-F) above to ensure visual consistency.

---

## III. Texture Direction

- **Flat-Color Realism** — The core of 90s hand-drawn animation: clear and fluid lines, uniform coloring, and distinct lighting layers.
- **Clear Line Art + Detailed Coloring** — This is the anchor for visual texture. Avoid blurring, smudging, or modern CG rendering styles.
- **Flat-Color Does Not Mean Simple** — This style emphasizes the tactile quality of 90s hand-drawn animation (clean line art, flat coloring, defined light and shadow). Amplify impact through composition and emotional design rather than relying on complex effects.
- **Hand-Drawn Does Not Mean Rough** — 90s Japanese animation emphasizes the cinematic expression of hand-drawn textures (natural light positioning, soft colors, delicate facial expressions). Amplify impact through lens language and emotional design rather than relying on spectacles.

---

## IV. Japanese Spatial Elements

Unique scene elements in 90s Japanese animation and their narrative functions:

- **Windows/Doorways/Corridors** — Natural frame-within-a-frame props that create depth and a sense of "hidden" space.
- **Classrooms/Rooftops/Streets** — Natural vessels for youth. The setting is the emotion: sunlight through a classroom window = youth; long shadows on a sunset street = nostalgia; warm light on a home dining table = domestic warmth.
- **Cherry Blossoms/Falling Leaves/Rain** — Natural carriers for "negative space" composition. Setting as emotion: full cherry blossoms = excitement; swirling leaves = sorrow; walking alone in the rain = loneliness.
- **Sunset/Moonlight/Streetlights** — The light sources for the genre. Sunset = warm/romance (Scheme D); Moonlight = cold/loneliness (Scheme E); Streetlights = nighttime daily life.
- **Use Establishing Shots for Transitions** — This style has a rich library of background assets (various times of day/weather variants). Use empty establishing shots for emotional buffering between segments; avoid hard cuts.
- **Use Visuals, Not Dialogue, for Turning Points** — Prioritize visual methods (lighting shifts, jump cuts, metaphorical empty shots) over explaining emotions through dialogue.

---

## V. Musical Instruments and Ambient Sound

Constraints on audio elements for the 90s Japanese animation world:

### Musical Instrument Selection

- **Piano** — The core instrument for daily life/warmth/memory segments; best at conveying the texture of 90s animation.
- **Guitar** — For relaxed/romantic/street scenes; the gentleness of fingerpicking is ideal for daily atmospheres.
- **String Layers** — For emotional catharsis/farewell segments; the delicacy of bowed strings is perfect for emotional transitions.
- **Music Box/Wind Chimes** — For memory/dreamy segments; clear, crisp tones fit a nostalgic atmosphere.

### Instrument Combination Strategy

| Emotional Phase | Instrument Combination |
|---|---|
| Stable/Opening/Ending | Solo Music Box or Solo Wind Chimes |
| Daily Warmth | Piano + Light Guitar |
| Emotional Catharsis/Romance | Strings + Piano |
| Memories/Dreamy | Music Box + Piano |

### Japanese Ambient Sound

- **Typical Ambient Layers** — Cicadas/insects / Train tracks / Wind through leaves / Rain on eaves / City chatter / Birdsong / Wind.
- **Label 1-2 core ambient sounds for every scene** to aid in subsequent sound design. The richer the layers of ambient sound, the more immersive the Japanese scene.
- **Silence is More Important Than Music** — For key emotional moments (eye contact, turning away, farewell), consider removing the score and keeping only ambient sounds (wind, cicadas, train noise), allowing the audience to feel the emotion themselves.
- **Avoid Over-Scoring** — Recommended soundtrack coverage is less than 50%. The "silence" in empty segments creates a sense of "breath" compared to the scored segments, highlighting genuine emotion.

# Storyboard Constraints: 90s Japanese Anime Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the director's core tool for translating a script into cinematic language. The following provides the constraint specifications for the 90s Japanese animation style at the storyboard level.

---

## II. Lighting and Atmosphere

- **Lighting Consistency within Scenes** — A single scene should not contain more than two lighting schemes unless there is a clear narrative shift (e.g., turning off a lamp leading to cool moonlight).
- **Emotional Palette Binding** — Each scene must be tied to at least one emotional setting (e.g., the thrill of meeting, daily warmth, or sentimental parting), maintaining a primary + secondary color continuity throughout the shot sequence.
- **Light Source Angle Specifications** — The default is 30-45° oblique side lighting to simulate natural light. When describing lighting, the distinction between frontal and side-angled lighting must be maintained.
- **Matching Color Temperature to Narrative Stages** — Maintain a warm tone for the first half (daily life/work/study); use cool tones (cool blue + warm contrast) during night scenes or following emotional turning points.
- **Lighting Transitions as Advanced Technique** — A gradual shift from natural light (A) to indoor warm light (B) denotes the passage of time from day to night. Mark specific points of lighting change within the storyboard.

---

## III. Environmental Dynamics

- **Environmental Dynamics for "Breathing" Room** — Incorporate elements such as swaying leaves, moving clouds, passing trains, falling rain, or flickering candlelight. Plan at least one shot with environmental dynamics every 3-4 shots to prevent the imagery from feeling "static."
- **Prioritize Japanese Environmental Elements** — Environmental dynamics should utilize elements consistent with the 90s Japanese anime worldview: falling cherry blossoms, swaying leaves, moving clouds, passing trains, falling rain. Elements that conflict with the 90s Japanese aesthetic are prohibited.

---

## IV. Animation Action Pacing

- **Natural Animation Flow** — All character actions should follow a natural pace by default. Actions like standing up, turning, or raising a hand should be labeled as "natural" or "gentle."
- **Facial Details** — Subtle expressions are a vital means of emotional expression in 90s Japanese anime. Include minor changes in the frame descriptions (a subtle smile, flickering eyes, a slight lowering of the head) to enhance the "liveliness" of the image.
- **Restraint and Subtlety** — Characters in this style should behave with restraint and subtlety; emotional expression should rely on details rather than exaggerated movements.

---

## V. Camera Movement Prohibitions

- **Disable Rapid Camera Movements** — Whip pans, rapid zooms, and handheld shaking conflict with the temperament of 90s Japanese animation (unless used specifically for transitions or emphasis).
- **Disable Fancy Transitions** — Screen wipes, rotations, shutters, and similar effects are incompatible with this style.
