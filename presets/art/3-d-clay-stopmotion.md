# Stop-Motion Claymation Style Guidelines

This style is specifically designed for "Stop-Motion Claymation" themes. All art prompts, specifications, and generated content are strictly limited to the following:

- **Stop-Motion Worldview**: Set against a backdrop of vintage/fantasy life, covering wooden cabins, forests, streets, cafes, gardens, etc., embodying the distinct atmosphere of life characteristic of stop-motion animation.
- **Clay Texture**: All characters, scenes, and props must adhere to the standards of stop-motion clay animation, emphasizing clear clay textures, visible finger indentations, and material graininess.
- **Healing Nostalgic Aesthetics**: On top of the clay texture, the style pursues a cinematic atmosphere with warm and soft color tones, rich details, and an overall feeling of warmth, healing, and nostalgia.
- **Emotional Expression**: Suitable for showcasing gentle, healing, and romantic stop-motion stories, emphasizing emotional expression through handcrafted textures.

## Scope of Application

- AI art generation for stop-motion claymation themes (characters, scenes, props, storyboards, etc.)
- Visual creation requiring exquisite clay textures, delicate lighting and shadows, and a warm, healing atmosphere.

## Prohibited Content

- Modern 3D animation styles, smooth plastic looks, or polished CG rendering
- Modern elements, modern architecture, or modern clothing
- High-saturation fluorescent colors or neon colors
- Dark, violent, or adult-oriented content

## Style Experience

Under this style, you will experience:

- Characters with refined features and gentle or clear temperaments, with clothing and styling in a stop-motion clay animation style.
- Scene spaces with rich layers, clear clay textures, and an atmosphere that is both realistic and poetic.
- Props, daily objects, and environmental details in vintage/fantasy designs with delicate, handcrafted textures.
- Storyboards and visuals based on stop-motion animation, emphasizing narrative and emotional expression.

---

This style is only applicable to projects related to "Stop-Motion Claymation." If other styles are required (such as modern 3D, CG, illustration, modern, etc.), please do not use this set of prompts.

## I. Layering Principles

1. **Unchanged Facial Features** — The facial features must be identical to the base model after layering.
2. **Unchanged Pose** — Maintain the base model's natural standing posture.
3. **Layer-by-Layer Control** — Describe each layer independently to facilitate per-layer replacement.
4. **Unified Style** — All clothing and makeup elements must adhere to the same clay aesthetic system.
5. **No Loss in Texture** — The clay texture standard after layering must not be lower than the base model.
6. **Costume/Makeup Domain Only** — Overlay only makeup/hair/clothing/accessories; props, scenes, and environments are strictly prohibited.

# Clay Constraint · Stop-Motion Clay Animation · Technique Reference

---

## I. Color System and Visual Tone

- **Color Foundation** — The entire film is based on Cream Yellow (C1), Terracotta Red (C4), and Sky Blue (C6). The overall color temperature is warm (4500-5000K) with low-to-medium saturation (60-75%), presenting the warm, nostalgic feel of vintage stop-motion animation.
- **Warm Tones Throughout** — Maintain a warm base throughout, with localized accents of cool colors (Pale Violet C7, Mint Green C8) to add depth. Transitions between cool and warm should align with the story arc, rather than being mixed randomly.
- **Palette-First Principle** — Sequence planning must first bind to emotional scenes (meeting/daily life/adventure/farewell, etc.) before determining the primary/secondary colors and lighting scheme to avoid "plot-correct but emotion-incorrect" color choices.
- **Prohibited Gamut** — High-saturation fluorescent colors, neon colors, and dark/black color systems are incompatible with this style.

---

## II. Lighting Scheme System

- **Lighting as Narrative** — 6 lighting schemes correspond to different emotional sequences. During the planning stage, the director should determine the tonal direction at the sequence level, rather than specifying it frame-by-frame.
- **Light Source Angle** — Default 30-45° side lighting, simulating small theater stage lighting effects.

| Lighting Scheme | Name | Color Tendency | Applicable Emotion |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Soft Diffusion | Cream Yellow base + even soft light | Daily warmth |
| B | Fantasy Warmth | Amber warm + magic light particles | Fantasy entrance |
| C | Warm Side Light | Warm yellow primary + localized warm light | Indoor daily life |
| D | Daylight Diffusion | Natural warm tones + shadow layers | Outdoor nature |
| E | Moonlight Cool | Pale blue primary + warm accents | Nighttime dreams |

- **Warm Tone Consistency** — Adjust flexibly based on scene requirements; cool tones (pale blue + warm contrast) should only be used in nighttime/dream sequences.
- **Atmospheric Mapping** — The atmospheric direction of every scene should map to one of the above lighting schemes (A-F) to ensure visual consistency.

---

## III. Textural Direction

- **Documentary Feel of Clay Texture** — The core of realistic stop-motion clay: visible fingerprints, clear clay folds, and distinct material graininess.
- **Clay Texture + Delicate Lighting** — This is the anchor for visual quality. It is not smooth plastic, nor is it modern CG rendering; it is the handmade texture of stop-motion animation.
- **Material Authenticity** — All costume, makeup, and prop materials must be believable: the grain of the clay, the wrinkles in fabric, the texture of wood, the luster of metal. Avoid "plastic feel" and "smooth CG feel."
- **Clay Does Not Equal Roughness** — This style emphasizes the clay texture expression of stop-motion (clear textures, fingerprints, clay folds), using lighting and motion design to amplify emotional impact rather than relying on complex visual effects.

---

## IV. Clay-Style Scene Space Elements

Specific scene elements unique to the clay-style worldview and their visual narrative functions:

- **Miniature Scenes/Small Props** — Naturally framed composition tools; small-scale scenes create a unique sense of layering and spatial depth in a "cute miniature world."
- **Warm Interiors/Handmade Props** — Natural carriers for negative space composition; handmade props add a "tangible feel": wooden table = warmth of home; flower pot = sense of living; books = knowledge/growth.
- **Light/Window Light/Candlelight** — The light carriers of the clay world; warm yellow light = warmth/safety (Schemes A/C), moonlight = fantasy/cool (Scheme E), window light = daily/natural (Scheme D).
- **Use Scene Establishers for Transitions** — This style features rich scene assets (variations in time/weather). It is recommended to use scene establishing shots as emotional buffers between sequences rather than jump-cutting.
- **Use Visuals, Not Dialogue, for Turning Points** — Prioritize visual means (lighting changes, shot size jumps, metaphorical establishing shots) combined with motion design, rather than relying on dialogue to explain the plot.

---

## V. Clay-Style Musical Instruments and Ambient Sound

Constraints on sound elements within the clay-style worldview:

### Musical Instrument Selection

- **Music Box** — The core instrument for daily/warm/nostalgic sequences, best at expressing the texture of stop-motion animation.
- **Accordion** — For relaxed/romantic/street sequences; the gentle strumming is suitable for daily atmospheres.
- **String Bed** — For emotional escalation/farewell sequences; the delicacy of bowing is suitable for emotional transitions.
- **Xylophone/Glockenspiel** — For fantasy/magic sequences; crisp tones are suitable for dreamy atmospheres.
- String beds can add a cinematic feel but should not overpower the scene.

### Instrument Combination Strategy

| Emotional Stage | Instrument Combination |
|---|---|
| Stable/Opening/Closing | Xylophone solo or Glockenspiel solo |
| Daily Warmth | Music Box + Accordion |
| Emotional Escalation/Romance | Strings + Music Box |
| Emotional Stability | Music Box solo |

### Clay-Style Ambient Sound

- **Typical Ambient Layers** — Footsteps (clay texture) / Page turning / Cutlery clinking / Wind / Clock ticking / Fabric friction.
- **Annotate 1-2 core ambient sounds for each scene** to assist in subsequent sound design. The richer the ambient layers, the more immersive the clay-style scene becomes.

# Storyboard Clay Constraints · Claymation · Technical Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the core tool for a director to translate a script into visual language. The following provides constraint specifications for the claymation style at the storyboard level.

---

## II. Lighting and Atmosphere

- **Lighting Consistency within Scenes** — No more than two lighting schemes should appear within a single scene, unless there is a clear narrative pivot (e.g., turning off a desk lamp → cool moonlight).
- **Emotional Color Palette Binding** — Each scene must be bound to at least one emotional setting (e.g., romantic encounter/warm daily life/melancholy farewell), maintaining primary and secondary color continuity across the shot sequence.
- **Light Source Angle Specifications** — Default to 30-45° side lighting to simulate theatrical lighting effects. When describing lighting, the difference between front and side lighting angles must be distinguished.
- **Matching Warm/Cool Tones to Narrative Stages** — Keep the first half (daily life/work/study) in warm tones; use cool tones (cool blue contrasted with warm colors) at night or after a narrative shift.
- **Lighting Transitions as a High-Level Technique** — Gradient from natural light (A) to indoor warm light (C) = the passage of time from day to night. Mark the change points of light and shadow in the storyboard.

---

## III. Environmental Dynamics

- **Environmental Dynamics Add Visual "Breathing"** — Falling leaves, flickering candlelight, swaying curtains, falling raindrops, magical light particles. Arrange at least one shot with environmental dynamics every 3-4 shots to prevent the frame from appearing "dead."
- **Prioritize Clay-Style Environmental Elements** — Environmental dynamics should utilize elements within the claymation world view: leaf movement, candle flicker, curtain swaying, subtle prop adjustments, and magical dust, reflecting the handcrafted texture and the dynamics of a miniature world.

---

## IV. Clay-Style Movement Rhythm

- **Claymation Movements Must Be Slow** — All character movements should default to a naturally slow rhythm. Actions such as standing up, turning around, or raising a hand should be labeled as "natural" or "gentle."
- **Clay-Texture Dynamics** — Clay characters naturally possess a "clumsy and cute" attribute. Subtle actions such as fingertip touches or slight head tilts can be added to shot descriptions to increase the "liveliness" of the frame.
- **Use Implication for Sweet/Romantic Interactions** — Eye contact, slight fingertip touches, or physical contact when handing over items. Avoid large-scale, two-person interactions; break them down into suggestive close-up shots.

---

## V. Camera Movement Prohibitions

- **No Rapid Camera Movements** — Whip pans, rapid zooms, and quick cuts conflict with the aesthetic of claymation.
- **No Fancy Transitions** — Wipes, spins, blinds, etc., are incompatible with this style.
