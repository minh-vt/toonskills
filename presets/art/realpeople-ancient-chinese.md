# Realistic Ancient Chinese Style Guide

This style is specifically designed for "Realistic Ancient Chinese" themes. All art prompts, specifications, and generated content are strictly limited to the following:

- **Ancient Worldview**: Set in ancient China, covering residences, palaces, artifacts, costumes, weaponry, etc. Modern elements are strictly prohibited.
- **Photorealistic Style**: All characters, scenes, and props must meet the standard of realistic photography, emphasizing extreme detail and authentic texture in facial features, skin, hair, and materials.
- **Cinematic Ancient Realism**: Building on a foundation of realism, the style pursues a cinematic atmosphere. It features strong contrast between light and shadow, rich details, and an overall ambiance that balances warmth within coolness and openness with density.
- **Sweet Romance Atmosphere**: Ideal for portraying gentle, healing, and romantic ancient love stories, emphasizing emotional expression through Eastern aesthetics.

## Scope of Application

- AI art generation for realistic ancient Chinese themes (characters, scenes, props, storyboards, etc.)
- Visual creations requiring extreme realism, delicate textures, and a classical Eastern atmosphere

## Prohibited Content

- Cartoon, anime, illustration, or "2D" (manga/ACGN) styles
- Modern elements, modern clothing, or modern scenes
- High-saturation fluorescent colors or neon colors
- Suggestive, revealing, or otherwise inappropriate content

## Style Experience

Under this style, you will experience:

- Exquisite facial features for characters, with a cool or gentle temperament, and authentic ancient clothing, makeup, and hairstyles.
- Scenes with rich spatial layers, ultra-clear textures, and an atmosphere that is both realistic and poetic.
- Props, weaponry, and accessories featuring ancient designs with intricate craftsmanship and premium textures.
- Storyboards and videos based on live-action realism, emphasizing narrative and emotional expression.

---

This style is exclusively for projects related to "Realistic Ancient Chinese" themes. Please do not use this set of prompts if you require other styles (such as illustration, anime, or modern themes).

# Character Derivative Asset Generation · Constraint Manual

---

## I. Overlay Principles

1.  **Unchanged Facial Features** — After overlaying, the facial features must be identical to the base model; facial shifting is strictly prohibited.
2.  **Unchanged Pose** — Maintain the base model's natural standing pose; any changes in pose, action, or posture are prohibited.
3.  **Layer-by-Layer Control** — Each layer is described independently to facilitate layer-specific replacement (e.g., change outfits without changing makeup).
4.  **Unified Style** — All clothing and styling elements must adhere to the same aesthetic system.
5.  **No Loss of Texture** — The texture quality after overlaying must not be lower than that of the base model.
6.  **Strictly Clothing/Styling Scope** — Only overlay makeup, hairstyles, clothing, and accessories; introducing props, scenes, environments, or actions is prohibited.

---

## II. Overlay Hierarchy

| Level | Content | Description |
|---|---|---|
| L0 | Base Model | The foundation image; do not modify. |
| L1 | Makeup (Decision Layer) | Analyze user cues first, then decide on intensity: "Basic / Light / Formal." |
| L2 | Hairstyle | Hair bun / tied hair / braided hair + hair accessories. |
| L3 | Undergarments | Replace the basic white inner layer. |
| L4 | Outerwear/Main Garment | Large-sleeved shirt / straight-line robe / cape, etc. |
| L5 | Accessories | Headpieces / earrings / necklaces / waist ornaments / jewelry. |

> **Scope Boundaries**: Character derivative assets consist only of L0–L5 layers (clothing and styling). They do not include props (umbrellas/swords/fans/books/lanterns, etc.), scenes/environments (indoor/outdoor/weather), or poses/actions (walking/looking back/raising hands). These fall under different asset categories.

## III. Makeup Constraints (L1)

### Strategy: From Base Model to Derivative Styling (Crucial)

> Although the base model may be unadorned, derivative assets must enter the styling process by default. The system should analyze styling requirements based on user cues and decide the intensity among basic, light, and formal makeup, rather than remaining unadorned.

### L1 Cue Analysis and Makeup Decision

| Step | Processing Content | Decision Result |
|---|---|---|
| S1 | Extract user cues: Facial state words, emotion words, intensity words. | Formulate makeup requirement summary. |
| S2 | Filter non-makeup cues: Props, scenes, actions, or posture words are not grounds for makeup application. | Prevent misjudgment. |
| S3 | Match makeup style matrix and assign intensity level. | Basic / Light / Formal. |
| S4 | Generate final L1 prompt. | Output conclusion only; no analysis process. |

### Mapping Cues to Makeup (Execution Guidelines)

| Cue Type | Typical Cues | L1 Decision |

1. **Spatial Consistency** — Architectural structure/layout/materials must remain consistent across all variants.
2. **Shot-Driven** — The same scene displays different narrative functions through different shot types.
3. **Time-of-Day Shifting** — The same space presents different lighting and atmospheric moods at different times.
4. **Weather Variations** — The same space conveys different emotions under various weather conditions.
5. **Anchored in Real Photography** — All variants must maintain a realistic photographic texture, avoiding 3D rendering/CG animation styles; preserve lens optical characteristics and physical lighting.

# Ancient Style Constraints · Realistic Ancient Live-Action · Technical Reference

---

## I. Color System and Visual Tone

- **Base Tone** — The entire film is based on Moon White (C1), Cold White Skin (C2), and Cyan/Damp Indigo (C6). The overall color temperature is cool (4800-5200K) with low-to-medium saturation (30-50%), presenting a sophisticated, melancholy, and chilled "high-end grey" tone.
- **Warm/Cool Narrative Contrast** — Warm colors (Amber Warm C7, Pearlescent Gold C3, Haze Pink C5) serve as visual signals for narrative shifts, used locally to accent segments where emotions thaw. The transition between cool and warm should sync with the story arc, not mixed randomly.
- **Warm/Cool Distribution Strategy** — Sorrowful/Isolated/Oppressive segments must strictly maintain the cool base tone; warm colors like orange or amber are prohibited. Warm colors are only released after emotional recovery, using the contrast to drive the narrative turning point.
- **Color Palette First Principle** — Segment planning must bind emotional scenes (First meeting/Warming up/Misunderstanding/Reunion/Wedding, etc.) before determining primary/secondary colors and lighting schemes, avoiding a situation where "the plot is right but the emotion is not color-coded."
- **Forbidden Color Gamut** — High-saturation fluorescent colors, neon colors, and modern color systems are incompatible with this style.

---

## II. Lighting Scheme System

- **Lighting as Narrative** — 7 lighting schemes correspond to different emotional segments. During the planning stage, the director should determine the lighting tone at the segment level, rather than specifying it frame by frame.
- **Light Angle** — Default 38.5° oblique angle for front-facing shots (not 45°); this can be adjusted appropriately for side shots.

| Lighting Scheme | Name | Color Tendency | Applicable Emotion |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Pearlescent Softness | Cold white base + subtle warm skin light | Daily warmth, relaxed and sweet |
| B | Side-lit Ethereal | Moon white + pearlescent gold rim light | Fairy-like appearance, stunning entrance |
| C | Candlelight Warmth | Amber warm dominance + ink/jade darks | Night intimacy, warming affection |
| D | Cold Moonlight | Cyan/Damp Indigo + frosty silver | Night solitude, longing |
| E | Translucent Window | Cold white base + dappled side light | Indoor daytime, daily life |
| F | Diffused Skylight | Cyan/Damp Indigo distance + moon white mist | Long shots, fog, atmospheric empty shots |
| G | Cold Side-light | Cyan/Damp Indigo cold white + 38.5° cool side-light, no warmth | Sorrow, isolation, oppressive coldness |

- **Warm/Cool Light Distribution** — Warm light (Amber Warm/Warm Orange) is for emotional recovery segments; sorrowful/isolated segments must use cool light exclusively. Directors may adjust the switching points based on narrative needs.
- **Atmospheric Mapping** — The atmosphere of every scene should map to one of the above lighting schemes (A-G) to ensure visual consistency.

---

## III. Texture Direction

- **Ultra-Clear Documentary Feel** — The core of realistic live-action photography: pores are visible, individual strands of hair are distinct, and texture details are ultra-sharp.
- **High Contrast + Extreme Detail** — This is the anchor of the visual texture. It is not film grain, not ink-wash aesthetics, but cinema-grade documentary photography.
- **Material Realism** — All costumes, makeup, and props must be believable: the luster of silk, the texture of cotton/linen, the reflection of metal, and the translucence of jade. "Plastic feel" and "CG feel" are prohibited.
- **Skin Texture** — Real skin pores, subtle blemishes, and natural luster. Avoid over-smoothing or excessively highlighting flaws; strive for "real but aesthetically pleasing."
- **Realism is not Dullness** — Realistic ancient live-action emphasizes tangible, shootable visual expressions (real light positions, real materials, real movement rhythms), amplifying impact through lens and emotional design rather than relying on spectacular effects.

---

## IV. Ancient Scene and Spatial Elements

Specific spatial elements and their visual narrative functions in an ancient-style worldview:

- **Sheer Curtains/Screens/Door Frames** — Natural framed composition props that create depth and "hidden" layers.
- **Courtyards/Flower Trees/Rain Screens** — Natural carriers for negative space (ma-ble) composition. Scenery equals emotion: Garden in full bloom = Relieved, Sitting alone in rain = Lonely, Falling leaves = Parting sorrow.
- **Candlelight/Moonlight/Window Light** — Carriers of light in the ancient world. Candlelight = Warm/Private (Scheme C), Moonlight = Cold/Loneliness (Scheme D), Window light = Daily/Quiet (Scheme E).
- **Transitions between segments** — Use scene "empty shots" (establishing shots). This style has rich asset variations (different times/weather); use empty shots for emotional buffering rather than harsh cuts.
- **Visuals over Dialogue for transitions** — Prioritize visual storytelling (abrupt changes in lighting, jump cuts, metaphorical empty shots) rather than relying on dialogue to explain.

---

## V. Ancient Musical Instruments and Ambient Sound

Sound element constraints for the ancient world view:

### Instrument Selection

- **Xiao (Vertical Bamboo Flute)** — Core instrument for bleak, lonely, and sorrowful segments. Best at expressing coldness and grief.
- **Erhu (Two-stringed fiddle)** — For emotionally turbulent, tragic, or longing segments. The crying tone of the strings is perfect for emotional outbursts.
- **Suona** — For segments with extreme emotional fluctuations (great tragedy/joy, fate shifts, climaxes). Use with caution; it is an "emotional nuke."
- **Guqin** — Used for opening/setting the tone or calm segments; pairs well with the Xiao.
- **Pipa (Lute)** — Used as accents for tense or hurried segments; not for leads.
- String pads can add a cinematic feel but should not overshadow the lead instruments.

### Instrument Combination Strategy

| Emotional Stage | Instrument Combination |
|---|---|
| Calm/Opening/Closing | Guqin solo OR Guqin + Xiao |
| Growing Sorrow | Xiao + Erhu |
| Emotional Outburst/Fate Shift | Suona solo OR Suona + Erhu |
| Tense/Hurried | Pipa accents + String background |

### Ancient Ambient Sounds

- **Typical Ambient Layers** — Cicada chirping / Babbling brooks / Wind through bamboo forests / Market cries / Night rain on eaves / Fabric friction / Light wind chimes.
- **Label 1-2 core ambient sounds for every scene** to assist in subsequent sound design. The richer the ambient layering, the more immersive the ancient scene will be.

## Lighting & Atmospheric Terminology (Realistic Ancient Style)

> **Design Principle**: Lighting is divided into two layers: **Ambient Light (Primary)** and **Equipment Light (Secondary)**. Ambient light is determined by the time of day, weather, and space, serving as the absolute tonal foundation of the image. Equipment light is used only for character sculpting and emotional reinforcement; it must obey the direction and color temperature of the ambient light and must not create a sense of an independent light source.

### I. Ambient Light (Primary Light Source · Natural Scene Light)

> Each storyboard **must first define the ambient light** before deciding whether to overlay equipment light.

#### Ambient Light by Time of Day

| Time | Natural Light Source | Tone Tendency | Atmospheric Vibe |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Early Morning | Diffused morning light, scattered skylight | Cold white tone, light blue tint | Misty, airy, dew-kissed texture |
| Afternoon | Slanted sunlight, diffused scattered light | Neutral tone, light warmth | Dappled light and shadows, clear layering |
| Dusk / Sunset | Slanted afterglow, alternating cold/warm light | Cold tone primarily, warm light at the horizon | Stretched shadows, poetic sense of light |
| Night | Cold blue moonlight, warm candle/lantern dots | Cold blue dominant | Deep shadows, high contrast between light and dark |
| Rainy / Cloudy | Diffused cold light, overcast with no main source | Grayish-cold tone | Damp feeling, low saturation |

#### Ambient Light by Space Type

| Space Type | Typical Ambient Light | Light/Shadow Characteristics |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Open Outdoors | Skylight dominant, sun direction dictates light/dark | Atmospheric perspective naturally creates depth |
| Courtyard/Garden | Skylight filtered through foliage, dappled scattering | Contrast between corridor shadows and courtyard light |
| Interior (Window) | Unidirectional natural window light, interior fades | Side-lighting, natural transition of light/dark |
| Secret/Dark Room | Localized warm candlelight/oil lamp, overall dark | Single source, limited range, deep shadows |
| Bamboo Forest | Light piercing through gaps, scattered light spots | Light from above, intersecting light/shadow on ground |
| Water Surface | Skylight + surface reflection | Sparkling water, shadow reflections, misty atmosphere |

### II. Equipment Light (Secondary Light · Character Sculpting)

> ⚠️ Equipment light **must follow the ambient light foundation**: direction must align with ambient source, color temperature must not conflict, and intensity must not exceed the ambient light.

#### Types of Equipment Light

| Light Type | Function | Application | Usage Constraints |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Rim Light | Outline the character, enhance separation from background | Backlight/Side-backlight environment | Intensity lower than ambient, match ambient color temp |
| Catchlight | Increase vitality and spark in the eyes | Close-up shots | Extremely subtle, only for focus, no obvious spots |
| Fill Light (Reflective) | Brighten dark details, avoid pitch-black faces | When side/backlight causes overly heavy shadows | Simulate environmental reflection, no independent source |
| Top-down Pressure | Create facial shadows, enhance oppressive feel | Interrogations, stand-offs, majestic scenes | Requires logical scene source (lanterns, skylights) |

#### Emotion → Ambient + Equipment Light Combination

| Emotional Base | Ambient Light (Primary) | Equipment Light (Secondary) | Principles |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Heartbeat/Warmth | Soft side-backlight, scattered warm ambient | Subtle rim light | Define backlight base; rim light only for contour |
| Stand-off/Pressure | Hard natural side light, high-contrast | Optional top light (if logical) | Use ambient for hardness; equipment light for contrast |
| Suppression/Sadness | Diffused cold light, cloudy/dark room | Minimal fill, keep dark parts | Keep cold ambient; omit fill to retain shadow layers |
| Mystery/Solemnity | Cold blue ambient, backlight silhouette | Precise edge light, restrained flare | Define cold base; edge light only for separation |
| Ethereal/Aesthetic | Scattered soft light, slight overexposure | Generally none | Scattered ambient, blurred distance, airy perspective |

### III. Conflict Avoidance Rules

1. **Define Ambient first, then add Equipment light.**
2. **Color Temperature Consistency**: Must coordinate with ambient light.
3. **Directional Logic**: Must be supported by logical sources within the scene.
4. **Intensity Subordination**: Equipment light intensity **must not exceed** ambient light.
5. **Quality over Quantity**: Do not add equipment light if ambient light is sufficient.

# Storyboard Constraints for Historical Drama · Live-Action Realistic Period Style · Technical Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the core tool used by the director to translate the script into visual language. The following guidelines provide constraints specifically for the live-action realistic period style within the storyboard framework.

---

## II. Lighting and Atmosphere

- **Unified Lighting within a Scene** — No more than two lighting schemes should appear within a single scene unless there is a clear narrative pivot (e.g., candles blown out → cold moonlight).
- **Emotional Color Palette Binding** — Each scene must be bound to at least one emotional state (e.g., initial spark/misunderstanding upon separation/reconciliation), maintaining consistency in primary and secondary colors across the shot sequence.
- **Light Source Angle Specifications** — The default for front-facing shots is a 38.5° oblique angle (rather than a generalized 45°); side-facing shots should be adjusted based on the needs of the frame. Distinguish between front and side lighting angles when describing lighting.
- **Matching Color Temperature to Narrative Stages** — Sadness/isolation/abandonment segments must strictly use cold lighting (cyan, dark blue, or cold white), and the use of warm orange/amber is prohibited. Warm tones (warm orange contrasting with cold blue backgrounds) should only be used after a warming of emotions.
- **Lighting Transitions as a High-Level Technique** — A fade from light filtering through window screens (E) to warm candle shadows (C) indicates the passage of time from day to night. Mark lighting transition points clearly in the storyboard.

---

## III. Environmental Dynamics

- **Environmental Dynamics for Visual Breath** — Use falling petals, rising mist, rippling water, or blowing gauze curtains. Incorporate at least one shot with environmental dynamics every 3–4 shots to prevent the frame from appearing "dead."
- **Prioritize Period-Specific Elements** — Environmental dynamics must utilize elements consistent with the historical world-building: falling flowers, catkins, light smoke, flowing water, wind through a bamboo forest, or flickering candlelight. Modern elements are strictly prohibited.

---

## IV. Historical Movement Rhythm

- **Slow Movements** — All character movements are "slow" by default. Actions such as rising, turning, or raising a hand should be annotated as "slowly."
- **Costume Dynamics** — The movement of historical attire (wide sleeves, flowing skirts, silk sashes) acts as a natural dynamic asset. Incorporate the movement of robes or skirts into shot descriptions to enhance the "liveliness" of the frame.
- **Etiquette Constraints** — The behavior of historical characters should reflect the temperament of the era: steady and slow walking, subtle and restrained gestures; avoid modern body language (e.g., shrugging shoulders, throwing hands up).

---

## V. Camera Movement Taboos

- **No Rapid Camera Movement** — Whip pans, rapid zooms, and handheld shaking conflict with the temperament of live-action realistic period dramas.
- **No Fancy Transitions** — Wipes, rotations, blinds, and similar effects are incompatible with this style.
