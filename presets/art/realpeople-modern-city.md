## II. Camera Syntax (Replacing "Rendering Engine")

> Live-Action Urban does not rely on rendering pipelines. Image quality depends on the choice of camera, the character of the lens, and the orchestration of light. Below are the core parameters for "Camera Syntax."

### 2.1 Aspect Ratio and Format

| Parameter | Baseline Value | Aesthetic Intent |
|---|---|---|
| Aspect Ratio | 1.85:1 (Widescreen) or 16:9 | Urban lateral sense of space, the coexistence of characters and environment; vertical (9:16) only for mobile narrative contexts. |
| Image Format | 35mm film texture / Full-frame digital camera | Shallow depth of field capability, natural vignette transitions, subtle grain structure. |
| Color Science | ARRI Alexa / Sony Venice-class cinematic color science | Natural skin tone reproduction, soft highlight roll-off, layered shadows without crushing. |

### 2.2 Lens Character

| Focal Length | Narrative Character | Typical Application |
|---|---|---|
| 24-28mm (Wide) | Environment consumes characters, spatial detachment, urban grandeur vs. individual insignificance | Urban establishing shots, loneliness in a crowd, relationship between architecture and people. |
| 35mm (Small Wide) | Subjective POV, immersive, documentary-style | Street following shots, indoor dialogue, walking through daily life. |
| 50mm (Standard) | Equivalent to human eye, objective gaze, no embellishment or distortion | Character portraits, two-person dialogue, emotional gazing. |
| 85mm (Medium Tele) | Compresses space, shortens emotional distance, background blur to highlight character | Close-ups, voyeuristic angles, seen from across the street. |
| 135mm+ (Tele) | Extreme compression, voyeurism, stripping characters from their environment | Isolated individuals in the city, distant views, physical distance of emotion. |

### 2.3 Camera Position and Movement

| Technique | Visual Psychology | Usage Scene |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed Position | Calm, observational, letting time speak for itself | Daily scenes, dialogue, negative space in establishing shots. |
| Handheld Micro-shake | Presence, breath-like quality, subjective intervention | Emotional fluctuations, street walking, intimate following. |
| Steadicam/Gimbal | Elegant flow, the poetry of urban wandering | Important scene transitions, character entrances, space exhibition. |
| High Angle | Sense of fate, insignificance, God's-eye view | City overlooking, reflecting on the ending, character dilemmas. |
| Low Angle | Oppression or sublimity | Oppression of skyscrapers, the power of characters. |
| Rack Focus | Shift of attention, change of relationships | Shifting focus during eye contact, revealing hidden information. |

## III. Urban Color Spectrum — Real Colors of Light and Material

> The colors of Live-Action Urban come from the real light and materials of Chinese cities, not from engine color palettes. The following palettes are extracted from: reflections on glass curtain walls of office buildings, the passage of time on cement walls of old residential areas, rainy nights under streetlamps, and fluorescent lights in convenience stores.

## IV. Global Constraint Rules

### 4.1 Image Authenticity Constraints (Mandatory)

| No. | Rule |
|---|---|
| P1 | Must use "Live-action photography / Real-life shot / Real-life film still" as the generation anchor — the image must read as "captured by a camera," not "generated by a computer." |
| P2 | Must specify a concrete light logic: Where does the light come from? (window/lamp/sky/screen) Direction, color temperature, and hardness must be self-consistent. |
| P3 | Must retain the true texture of human skin — pores, fine lines, moles, natural skin tone unevenness — strictly forbid plastic skin-smoothing and AI-fake masks. |
| P4 | Hair must appear in a natural state — wind-blown, rain-wet, loose strands after being tied — strictly forbid modeling-like hair blocks. |
| P5 | Clothing must show signs of "wear" — fabric wrinkles, slight pilling, natural collar deformation, frayed cuffs — strictly forbid the look of brand-new, sample clothes. |
| P6 | Scenes must provide evidence of being "used" — objects scattered on desks, traces of life on walls, imperfections on floors — strictly forbid "model home" aesthetics. |
| P7 | Characters must be in natural postures — real standing (weight shifted to one side), spontaneous expressions (non-symmetrical smiles), daily hand movements. |
| P8 | Must specify a concrete contemporary Chinese urban space — refuse "generic city" or any stateless, fictionalized metropolis. |

### 4.2 Cinematographic Quality Constraints (Mandatory)

| No. | Rule |
|---|---|
| Q1 | Must declare the "presence mode" of the camera: fixed gaze / handheld breathing / gimbal flow / telephoto voyeurism. |
| Q2 | Depth of field must have narrative intent — shallow DOF to highlight emotion, deep DOF to emphasize environment, rack focus to guide attention. |
| Q3 | Highlights must not be overexposed to dead white, and shadows must not be crushed to black without layers — retaining highlight roll-off and shadow detail is the foundation of cinematic feel. |
| Q4 | Must declare preference for grain texture: 35mm film grain (fine and irregular) / Digital clean sharp (default). |
| Q5 | Color tonality must match the emotional scene — warm without bleeding colors, cold without being muddy, neon without looking cheap. |

### 4.3 Prohibited Items (Highest Priority, Non-Overridable)

| No. | Prohibited Content |

| X3 | Prohibited: "Ancient style / Costumed / Hanfu / Xianxia / Wuxia / Republican era / Ancient architecture" and all non-contemporary Chinese urban elements. |

### 4.4 Style Deviation Judgment

> If any of the following signals appear in your generated images, you have deviated from the Live-Action Urban style:

| Deviation Signal | Description |
|---|---|
| Skin looks like silicone | Over-smoothed, losing the texture of a real person — stop using words like "smooth" or "perfect skin" immediately. |
| Light source is unclear | Image is bright but the light origin is undefined — complete the light logic, reject "globally uniform brightness." |
| Architecture lacks Chinese branding | City loses Chinese identity — add shop signs, street signs, bus stops, and other clear Chinese urban symbols. |
| Image is too clean | Scene looks like it was just renovated — add signs of life. |
| Character posture like a model | Stiff and symmetrical posture — provide a natural shift in body weight. |
| Color like a filter app | Tonalities are uniform but look cheap — return to light color-temperature driving, do not use "filter presets." |

# Visual Manual for "Real-Life Urban" Subjects · Style Guide

This manual is specifically crafted for visual creation in the "Real-Life Urban" genre. All art prompts, visual specifications, and generated content are strictly limited to the aesthetic system of live-action urban cinematography.

---

## I. Core Definition of the Style

**Real-Life Urban** is a visual style system based on **live-action cinematic photography aesthetics**, with **contemporary Chinese urban life** at its core. Its key characteristics are:

- **Live-Action Photorealistic Texture**: Anchored in live-action film-grade photography, the imagery presents authentic human skin textures, hair details, micro-expressions, and body language. It strictly avoids 3D-modeled, CG-rendered, or hand-drawn aesthetics.
- **Contemporary Urban Worldview**: All scenes are strictly limited to contemporary Chinese urban spaces—office buildings, residential areas, streets, shopping districts, subways, parks, campuses, hospitals, and other authentic urban life scenarios.
- **Cinematic Photography Language**: Based on theatrical live-action film standards, emphasizing the organic coordination of natural and artificial light, professional narrative composition, depth-of-field and focus for emotional expression, and cinematic color grading.
- **Urban Humanistic Warmth**: While maintaining professional image quality, it emphasizes the "vibrancy of life" (the hustle and bustle), authentic emotional connections between people, and the aesthetic refinement of daily moments.

### Creative Philosophy and Core Value

The core creative philosophy of this manual is **"Reality is Power"**:

1. **Aesthetic Return to Reality**: In an era of overflowing AI-generated imagery, the photorealistic texture of live-action, with its irreplaceable power of reality, becomes the most emotionally resonant visual language. The texture of skin, the warmth in eyes, the folds in clothing, and the fragments of light passing through hair—these authentic details constitute a sense of life that 3D rendering cannot replicate.

2. **The Narrative Container of the City**: The contemporary city is the most familiar narrative space for the Chinese people. The cubicles of office buildings hold workplace dreams, the stairwells of old residential buildings echo with local life, the eye contact between strangers on the subway, the single light in a late-night convenience store—the urban space itself is a massive narrative container waiting to be explored.

3. **Humanistic Visual Stance**: This manual insists on "people" as the core. No matter how grand the scene or how gorgeous the lighting, it must eventually return to the emotional state and inner world of the characters. Every frame is a gaze upon humanity.

4. **The Critical Point between Cinema and Daily Life**: Seek a delicate balance between "cinematic refinement" and "everyday reality"—the image should be beautiful and professional enough to have a cinematic feel, but not so overly polished that it loses the sense of life; characters should be real and vivid enough, but not so casual that they lose aesthetic quality.

**Core Values:**

- Providing a systematic and reusable methodology for AI visual creators focusing on urban subjects.
- Establishing a standardized visual creation pipeline from concept to final product.
- Lowering the technical barriers and trial-and-error costs for AI-generated real-life urban content.
- Filling the current void in the AI visual creation field regarding a "systematic methodology for real-life urban subjects."

## III. Visual Style Positioning

### 3.1 Overall Style Positioning

**"Cinematic Real-Life Urban Aesthetic"**

Using the contemporary Chinese city as the narrative stage and live-action cinematography as the technical anchor, we pursue the image quality and aesthetic heights of theatrical urban films. The overall style seeks a golden balance between "cinematic feel" and "sense of life"—the image is refined but not hollow, realistic but not casual, aesthetically pleasing but not ostentatious.

### 3.2 Image Texture Tiers

| Texture Dimension | Positioning Standard |
|---|---|
| **Skin Texture** | Photorealistic; natural skin texture, pores, fine lines, and blemishes are visible. Avoid excessive skin smoothing or "plastic" looks. |
| **Hair Texture** | Clearly defined strands; naturally fluffy or flowing. Clear distinction between light-facing and back-lit areas. Avoid stiff "clumps" of hair. |
| **Fabric Texture** | Distinguishable fabric textures (cotton/linen weave, wool fuzz, silk luster, leather grain). Folds must respect body movement and gravity. |
| **Environmental Texture** | Walls show usage, floors show natural wear, glass shows reflection/refraction, metal shows oxidation and scratches. Avoid the "brand new prop" look. |
| **Lighting Texture** | Organic coordination of natural and artificial light. Avoid harsh contrasts and unnatural noise, pursuing cinematic color science. |

## IV. Key Visual Elements

### 4.1 Character Visual Elements

Characters in real-life urban subjects focus on **"Authentic individuals, urban temperament"**:

- **Facial Standards**: Realistic skin texture; natural, three-dimensional features; makeup that matches urban aesthetics without being overly stylized. Supports diverse types—cool/intellectual, sunny/energetic, mature/steady, cool/capable, gentle/healing.
- **Hairstyle Standards**: Contemporary urban trendy styles, natural hair texture. No "wiggy" or stiff looks. Supports long straight hair, collarbone-length cuts, sharp short hair, lazy curls, professional low ponytails, etc.
- **Clothing Standards**: Contemporary daily urban wardrobe—professional attire (suits, shirts, trench coats), casual wear (hoodies, knitwear, jeans), urban trends (designer pieces, layering), home wear (comfy knit loungewear). Clear fabric texture, natural folds.
- **Expression and Posture**: Authentic micro-expressions (gaze, corners of the mouth, brows), natural body language, avoiding "posed" stiffness or overly dramatic expressions.
- **Temperament Dimensions**: Elite/Capable | Gentle/Healing | Youthful/Energetic | Mature/Reserved | Cool/Independent | Local/Rustic | Artistic/Melancholic.

### 4.2 Scene Visual Elements

- **Office/Workspace**: Glass curtain walls, open cubicles, private offices, conference rooms, pantries—transparency of glass partitions, cool diffused light from screens, striped shadows from afternoon sun through blinds.
- **Urban Streets**: Old alleys, busy commercial districts, footbridges, subway stations, bus stops—shadows of trees, motion blur of traffic, the feeling of crowds/strangers, city texture from street signs and shop windows.
- **Home Space**: Old apartment stairwells, high-rise balconies, living rooms/kitchens/bedrooms—warmth of sunlight, traces of daily life (clutter), airiness of moving curtains.
- **Dining Space**: Local noodle shops, trendy cafes, late-night convenience stores, night markets, corporate canteens—the "smoke and fire" of food, mist on glass, the embrace of warm lighting.
- **Public Spaces**: Park benches, hospital corridors, school playgrounds, bookstore corners, rooftops—the resonance between space and character emotion.

### 4.3 Prop and Detail Elements

- **Urban Life Props**: Smartphones (notification pop-ups, screen reflections), laptops (keyboard light reflecting on face), coffee cups (steaming), headphones (symbol of solitude), umbrellas (core prop for rain), employee ID badges, food delivery packaging.
- **Lighting Props**: Desk lamps, streetlights, car headlights, neon signs, screen light, candles—all atmospheric builders in urban narrative.
- **Emotional Symbol Props**: Old photos, letters, rings, tickets, lunch boxes, potted plants—small items carrying narrative function.
- **Season/Weather Elements**: Spring cherry blossoms/willow catkins, summer rain/cicadas, autumn leaves/trench coats, winter breath/snowflakes—seasonality enhances the time dimension of urban narrative.

### 4.4 Camera Language Elements

| Shot Size | Narrative Function | Real-Life Urban Characteristics |
|---|---|---|
| Extreme Close-up | Emotional climax, key details. | Eyes, finger twitches, teardrops, message screens. |
| Close-up | Character emotion, conversational response. | Facial micro-expressions, shoulders up, emphasizing emotion transfer. |
| Medium Close-up | Character interaction, action narrative. | Waist up, balancing expressions and gestures. |
| Medium Shot | Character relations, spatial context. | Full body visible, relationship between character and environment. |
| Wide Shot | Environmental atmosphere, solitude/belonging. | Characters occupy a small portion, spatial narrative priority. |
| Establishing/Empty | Emotional buffer, metaphorical negative space. | No characters, pure environment, urban landscape. |

## V. Prohibited Content (Highest Priority)

This style strictly excludes the following content types to ensure a pure and unified visual system:

1. **Non-Realistic Textures**: 3D rendering/CG modeling/next-gen game engines/animation styles/hand-drawn illustrations/watercolors/oil paintings/anime/Voxel/low-poly—all non-live-action photorealistic qualities.
2. **Non-Urban Worldview**: Traditional costumes/period dramas/traditional Chinese ancient architecture/Xianxia/fantasy/Wuxia/Republic era/Western fantasy/medieval/Cyberpunk/sci-fi future/interstellar space—any non-contemporary urban background.
3. **Non-Contemporary Chinese Context**: Architecture, streets, signs, or character styles that are clearly non-Chinese (e.g., kimonos, Hanoks, European towns).
4. **Technical Defects**: Low resolution/blur/noise/deformation/malformed limbs/distorted facial features/poor proportions/poor cutouts/plastic looks/over-smoothing/fake-mask looks.
5. **Aesthetic Violations**: Over-saturation/neon "death" colors/cheap filter looks/excessive HDR/dirty/messy imagery/deliberately "creepy" tones.
6. **Content Compliance Redlines**: Nudity/vulgarity/suggestiveness/violence/gore/illegal content/discriminatory expression/politically sensitive elements.

## I. Casting Logic — The Person Before the Camera

> The starting point of a 3D character is "building a model"; the starting point of a live-action urban character is "choosing a person to stand before the camera." The following principles do not stem from modeling, but from casting and photography.

1. **The Face is the Narrative** — A face is an unwritten story. Live-action urban characters do not seek "perfect features," but rather "features worth gazing upon"—recognizable asymmetry, subtle imperfections that tell a story, and faces capable of holding a camera's gaze.
2. **"Ordinary Person" Over "Star Quality"** — Urban storytelling requires people who "look like someone you would see on the subway every day," rather than "someone who just stepped off a magazine cover." Authentic, makeup-free faces take precedence over polished, stylized looks.
3. **Posture Tells the Truth** — A natural standing posture is not symmetrical—the center of gravity shifts to one leg, shoulders are slightly tilted, and the neck leans or tilts naturally. Avoid model-like symmetrical stances or military-style attention stances.
4. **Consistency Across Angles** — The front, side, back, and close-up angles must present as a photographic record of the same individual, not four similar-looking people. Core identification markers: jawline contour, bridge of the nose transition, ear shape, and hairline trajectory.

## I. Styling Logic—Styling a Real Person

> "Real-life urban" does not concern itself with "material layering," "PBR rendering," or "modeling precision." Here, we discuss: a makeup artist working on a real face, a hair stylist using real tools on real hair, and a stylist picking a worn garment off a rack—and the camera capturing all of it.

1.  **Makeup is a "second skin," not a "face texture"** — Foundation blends with skin oils, eyeliner shifts slightly with the eye shape, and lipstick shows uneven textures due to lip lines—makeup must possess the "freshly applied" realism.
2.  **Hair is alive** — Styled hair still has stray strands, roots have natural volume rather than a wig cap look, and tightly tied ponytails show the natural tension on the scalp.
3.  **Clothes are "worn on the body," not "worn on a model"** — Shoulder lines aren't necessarily perfectly symmetrical (real human standing postures are asymmetrical), fabrics show natural wrinkles from body movement, and collars show slight deformation from being put on and taken off.
4.  **Styling serves the face, not masks it** — The worst styling is one that makes the base model unrecognizable. Derivative styling should enhance, not conceal, the core temperament of the character.

## I. Prop Design Principles

> Props in a live-action urban setting are not "modeled objects," but "used items"—they have spent time in someone's hands, rested on a specific desk, or been carried in a pocket. The camera captures them in this current moment.

## I. Derivative Principles

> The derivation of real-life urban props is not a "material swap," but rather "what the item has become when captured by the camera again at a different time." Wear and tear is a diary written by time on an object; stains are the signatures left by life.

1. **Shape Anchoring** — The core shape and silhouette of the prop must remain recognizable across all states. A phone used for two years might have screen scratches and dented frames, but its model must be immediately identifiable.
2. **State Readability** — New/old, intact/damaged, dry/wet—these must be distinguishable at a glance. State differences must stem from the physical logic of real life.
3. **Narrative Service** — The state of the prop is the "unfilmed backstory." A shattered phone implies an argument, water-soaked letters imply a heavy storm, and yellowed photos imply time locked away.
4. **Progressive Rationality** — Damage and aging follow the laws of physics for real materials: paper yellows before becoming brittle and tearing; metal oxidizes before rusting; fabric fades before wearing and tearing.
5. **Single-View Still Life Photography** — Derivative states only require one still life photograph. It must maintain the same camera angle, lighting, and background as the original prop reference—only the state of the prop changes.

## I. Aesthetic Principles of Scenes

> A realistic urban scene is not a "modeled space," but a "real location selected by the camera lens." No rendering engines, no texture maps, no volumetric light parameters—only real light, authentic materials, and the daily life of Chinese cities.

1. **Space is Narrative** — An empty office is not just "empty"; it is "just finished work." A crooked chair, a half-cold cup of coffee, an open document on a screen—the space tells its own story.
2. **Real Light, Not Rendered Light** — Scene light must have a single source: actual sunlight (through windows or door frames) or actual artificial light (fluorescent tubes, desk lamps, streetlights, neon signs). The color temperature and direction of the light must be physically logical.
3. **Used Spaces** — Realistic scenes are never pristine. There is clutter on desks, dust in corners, fingerprints on glass, and wear on floors. A space devoid of signs of life is a model home for a sales office, not a realistic urban environment.
4. **Authentic Contemporary Chinese Urban Sites** — Scenes must feature Chinese signage, Chinese urban characteristics (storefront signs, street signs, architectural styles, specific tree species). This is not a "generic urban" setting; it is a "specific corner of a contemporary Chinese city."
5. **Single-View Panorama** — One photograph captures the full information of the entire site. Use wide-angle or standard focal lengths with full depth of field to showcase the complete layout and depth of the space in a frontal view—no split-view quadrants, no multi-angle collages.

# Derivative Asset Generation for Live-Action Urban Scenes · Constraint Manual

## I. Principles of Derivation

> Deriving live-action urban scenes is not about "cutting camera angles for a rendered scene," but rather "photographing the same physical location from a different position, at a different time, and under different weather conditions."

1. **Spatial Anchoring** — The core spatial structure of the same scene must remain recognizable across all variants. Even with changes in angle, lighting, or weather, it must be immediately clear that "it is still the same place."
2. **Focal Length Storytelling** — Using different focal lengths on the same scene conveys different narratives. Wide-angle tells the story of "an individual's lonely relationship with the city," medium-angle highlights "this specific corner," and telephoto reveals "what lies in the distance."
3. **Time of Day is Mood** — An office in the morning versus the same office at midnight represents two different emotional states. When the time changes and the light shifts, the narrative function of the scene changes as well—it is not as simple as swapping a sky texture.
4. **Weather is Story** — On the same street, a sunny day represents routine, a rainy day evokes melancholy, and a snowy day signifies romance or loneliness. Weather acts as an emotional filter for the scene; it is not merely an overlay—it represents real optical and environmental changes.
5. **Single-View Photography** — Each derived variant must be a standalone panoramic photograph. While maintaining the same space as the original reference image, it can feature a different focal length, time of day, or weather condition.

## I. Color Palette and Visual Tone

- **Color Foundation** — The entire film is based on Urban White (#F5F2EC), Fog Gray (#9EA2A8), and Navy Blue (#2D3A4A). The overall color temperature is neutral-to-warm during the day (5000-5800K) and a mix of warm/cold at night (warm streetlights at 2800-3200K + cold environment at 6000-8000K). Saturation is mid-to-low (35-55%), presenting a restrained urban tone that is "cinematic, not filtered"—colors originate from actual light sources, not post-production grading.
- **Light as a Palette** — Instead of "primary/secondary" color pairings, drive screen colors via the color temperature and direction of light sources. Morning light 3500-4500K (warm white), midday 5500-6500K (clear cold white), golden hour 2800-3500K (warm gold), blue hour 8000-10000K (deep blue-violet), sodium streetlights 2000-2200K (warm orange-yellow), convenience store fluorescent lamps 4000-5000K (cold white).
- **Warm/Cold Narrative Contrast** — Warm light (morning/sunset/desk lamps/sodium streetlights) is used for warmth, healing, intimacy, and daily life segments. Cold light (midday skylight/overcast diffusion/blue hour/fluorescent lamps) is used for restraint, alienation, loneliness, and suspense. Key transitions can imply emotional shifts through changes in light source temperature.
- **Light Source First Principle** — During sequence planning, determine "where the light comes from and its color temperature" before setting the frame's color. Avoid "the shot looks good but the lighting makes no sense."
- **Restricted Color Gamut** — High-saturation neon colors, cheap filtered color grading (e.g., teal/orange presets, sepia presets), CG neon light spills, or abnormal color shifts not produced by real light sources.

## II. Lighting Scheme System

- **Lighting as Narrative** — 7 lighting schemes correspond to different emotional sequences. During the director's planning phase, define the lighting tone at the sequence level rather than specifying per shot. Each scheme must designate "Light Source + Color Temperature + Lighting Ratio" to ensure it can be physically simulated by Seedance 2.0.
- **Live-action Photography Lighting Characteristics** — Natural window light scheduling, practical lights (desk lamps/streetlights/screens/neon/car lights), environmental reflections (wet ground/glass curtain walls/water surfaces), handheld camera's slight breathing motion, and depth-of-field focus guidance—these are the core lighting tools for the live-action urban style.

| Scheme | Name | Lighting Formula | Light Ratio | Applicable Emotion |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| A | Urban Gilt | Sunset window light at 45° (3000-3500K) + fill light (sky reflection 5500K) | 1:3 Medium contrast | Peak moments, elite settings, warm memories, important meetings |
| B | Day Clarity | Large-area window light/diffused skylight (5000-5500K), no hard shadows, softened by curtains | 1:1.5 Low contrast | Daily relaxation, urban strolling, healing, office day |
| C | Warmth of Life | Warm-toned key light from desk/pendant/sodium streetlight (2800-3200K), details preserved in shadows | 1:4 Warm wrap | Home life, mundane daily life, warm beats, late-night intimacy |
| D | Cold Sharp | Lateral hard light (cold white window light 5000K or cold streetlight) + deep shadows | 1:8 High contrast | Urban suspense, confrontation, coldness, high-pressure moments |
| E | Interior Diffusion | Natural window diffusion (4500-5500K) + interior ceiling light/cold screen fill, soft shadows | 1:2 Soft transition | Interior day, office work, solitude, healing quiet |
| F | Rainy Night | Wet ground reflecting streetlights (warm 2800K spots + cold 6000K environment), window glass raindrop scattered light | 1:6 Mixed warm/cold | Walking alone in rain, longing, melancholic, suspense buildup |
| G | Blue Hour Poem | Sky diffusion 20 minutes after sunset (8000-10000K deep blue-violet) + initial artificial warm light spots | 1:5 Cold dominant, warm spots | Transitional moments, inner monologue, poetic white space, ending |

- **Warm/Cold Allocation** — Warm light (morning/sunset/desk/sodium streetlights) for warmth, healing, elite success, and daily life. Cold light (midday/overcast/blue hour/fluorescent) for restraint, alienation, suspense, and cold solitude. The warm/cold transition point is the narrative turning point.
- **Atmosphere Mapping** — Every scene's atmosphere should map to one of the schemes (A-G). If a hybrid scheme is required (e.g., F+G), specify the dominant light source.
- **Seedance 2.0 Lighting Adaptation Points**: Light source names should be AI-understandable ("Sunset window light" is better than "warm volumetric light"). Light ratio numbers help the model build a sense of light and dark. For multi-light source scenes, specify the hierarchy of Key Light + Fill Light + Ambient Light.

## III. Texture Direction — The Real World in Front of the Lens

> The only yardstick for live-action urban texture: material behavior captured by the camera, not parameters generated by a rendering engine.

- **Skin Texture** — Visible pores, natural T-zone oil/reflection, natural redness on cheekbones, slight darkness around the eyes—evidence of "living" skin. In Seedance 2.0 prompts, use "visible skin pores, natural skin texture, not airbrushed" to replace 3D project terms like "subsurface scattering/SSS."
- **Hair Texture** — Flyaway hairs naturally scattered on the forehead and neck, natural aggregation and separation of strands, translucent warm silhouettes under backlight, natural states when windblown or damp. In Seedance 2.0, use "flyaway hair strands, natural hair movement, backlit hair rim light" to replace "physically accurate hair simulation."
- **Clothing Texture** — Soft micro-wrinkles in cotton, twill texture and color fading in denim, looped patterns in knitwear, patina and bending in leather. Clothing shows evidence of "being worn"—naturally wrinkled hemlines, wear on cuffs. In Seedance 2.0, use "fabric texture with subtle wear, natural fabric drape, lived-in clothing."
- **Architectural Material** — Water stains and micro-cracks on concrete walls, reflections and fingerprints on glass curtain walls, worn patina on metal railings, slight scratches on tile floors, signs of use on wood surfaces. In Seedance 2.0, use "weathered building materials, real urban surfaces, not showroom clean."
- **Age Texture Above All** — Materials must not be too clean or perfect. Signs of life (daily clutter on a table, residual wall stickers, floor wear) are not defects; they are the foundation of spatial narrative. "Brand new showroom" and "spotless renderings" are prohibited.
- **Seedance 2.0 Texture Adaptation Points**: Avoid CG terms like "PBR material," "physically accurate rendering," or "8K map." Switch to "real texture, natural material surface, visible wear and use marks, not CGI." When describing slight flaws, use "subtle" rather than modeling terms like "micro-detail."

## IV. Live-action Urban Spatial Elements

Contemporary Chinese urban specific scene elements and their visual narrative functions in Seedance 2.0 video:

- **Windows/Floor-to-ceiling Windows/Glass Partitions** — Core composition and lighting tools. The window is the light entrance—the direction and color temperature of window light dictate the interior lighting logic. Glass partitions create multi-layered spaces of "separation and transparency" (foreground → glass → middle ground → glass → background). In Seedance 2.0, emphasize "light entering through window at specific angle" to help the model understand light direction.
- **Streets/Trees/Crosswalks/Traffic** — The spatial skeleton of outdoor scenes. Empty long street = loneliness; bustling traffic = urban alienation; wet road reflecting streetlights after rain = emotional density. In Seedance 2.0, street scenes must specify optical descriptions the model can execute, such as "wet road surface reflecting streetlights" or "dry pavement with long afternoon shadows."
- **Streetlights/Window Light/Screen Light/Car Lights** — Core narrative carriers of urban light. Warm yellow streetlights (2000-2200K sodium or 3000K LED) = warmth of the night; convenience store cold white fluorescent lights (4000-5000K) = safe island in the late night; blue phone screen light on a face = solitary companionship in moments of solitude.
- **Old District Alleys/High-rise Apartments/Office Buildings/Subway Stations** — Four narrative containers of urban architecture. Mottled walls and overhead wires in old districts = memories of daily life; high-rise floor-to-ceiling windows overlooking the city = elite loneliness or control; layered reflections in office glass partitions = workplace order and alienation; subway station fluorescent cold light + tunnel darkness = pauses in urban flow.
- **Use Empty Shots for Transitions** — Empty shots act as emotional buffers; avoid hard cuts. Empty shots of the same space at different times/weather (e.g., rain-slicked street → sunny street) can imply the passage of time. In Seedance 2.0, empty shots require specific light source logic; even empty shots have emotions.
- **Use Visuals, Not Dialogue, for Turning Points** — Light transitions (window light blocked by clouds → space suddenly dims), cut-ins (medium shot → macro close-up), spatial transitions (indoor → street view), focus shifts—let the camera complete the narrative.

## V. Live-action Urban Soundtrack and Ambient Sound

### 5.1 Instrument Selection

Live-action urban soundtracks favor acoustic instruments with restrained electronic elements, seeking a "presence that does not steal the spotlight":

- **Piano** — Core instrument for solitude, tenderness, and delicate segments. Best played with repeated single notes or sparse chords—the silence between notes is as important as the notes themselves.
- **Strings** — Driving force for emotional accumulation and release. Bass/mid-range for grounding (warm but not oppressive), high range for light, fleeting touches at emotional peaks.
- **Acoustic Guitar** — Baseline for daily relaxation, healing, and strolling. Texture of fingerpicking or light strumming, carrying the warmth of "afternoon sun."
- **Electric Guitar (Clean/Slight Overdrive)** — Emotional amplifier for urban nightlife, street wandering, or mild melancholy. Single-note melodic lines or atmospheric arpeggios; use distortion with caution.
- **Electronic Pad** — Low-frequency underlay for suspense, nightlife transitions, or time passing. An underlying presence that is "ignorable but wrong if removed."
- **Harmonica/Accordion** — Accent instruments for everyday life or nostalgic memories. Use sparingly; local appearance is a narrative signal.
- **Light Electronic Beat** — Rhythmic baseline for urban moments (commute montages, rapid city night cuts). Breakbeat or minimalist electronic drums, not exceeding 80 BPM.
- **Avoid**: Grand orchestral tutti, heavy metal distortion, high-energy EDM—these turn live-action urban into "music acting out the plot."

### 5.2 Soundtrack Combination Strategy

| Emotional Stage | Instrument Combination |
|---|---|
| Stable/Daily Narrative | Piano solo OR Piano + light electronic Pad |
| Healing/Relaxation | Acoustic guitar + Piano + light strings |
| Workplace Elite | Piano + mid-high strings + light electronic beat |
| Solitude/Longing | Sparse piano notes OR Piano + harmonica accent |
| Emotional Turn/Fate | String section crescendo + piano wrap-up |
| Suspense/Night | Electronic Pad + light beat + electric guitar clean notes |
| Daily Life/Nostalgia | Acoustic guitar + accordion/harmonica accent + light strings |
| Ending/Aftertaste | Piano single notes fading out → Ambient sound solo |

### 5.3 Live-action Urban Ambient Sound

> Ambient sound is the "auditory texture" of urban scenes, defining immersion. Label 1-2 core ambient sounds per scene.

**Core Ambient Sound Layers:**
- **Indoor**: Air conditioner hum/keyboard typing/elevator prompts/dripping faucets/refrigerator compressor/wind on curtains/ticking clocks.
- **Outdoor**: Traffic tire noise/distant voices/wind through buildings/rustling leaves/birdsong/rain on car windows/muffled construction sounds/shared bike lock sounds.
- **Transitional**: Subway station broadcasts and screeching brakes/mall background music and crowds/elevator operation and door chimes/footsteps in corridors from far to near.
- **Silence as Ambient Sound**: Low-frequency hum of a fridge in a late-night apartment, 5 AM streets with no traffic—this "absence of sound" is itself a narrative.

Sound Design Philosophy:
- Ambient sound isn't pasted on; it belongs to the scene.
- "Removing" ambient sound at critical moments is more emotionally impactful than "adding" sound effects.
- Changes in ambient sound imply spatial transitions—entering a quiet convenience store from a noisy street makes the sound suddenly "cleaner."

## VII. Global Narrative Constraints

- **Empty Shots are Emotions** — Use empty shots (same space in different light) as buffers between narrative beats. Empty shots are not "nothing to film," they are "giving the audience a breather."
- **Continuity and Change of Light** — Window light angle in the same space must change continuously during daytime scenes. If a conversation spans 30 minutes, the window light position should shift—Seedance 2.0 understands this "logical inconsistency."
- **Consistent Face Lighting** — Faces of all characters in a shot must have the same light source direction. If key light is from the left, left faces must be bright.
- **Avoid "Over-directing"** — Urban live-action seeks "life captured by the camera," not "plot arranged by a screenwriter." Allow uncontrollable daily details (wind moving curtains, a passing cat, slight wobbles in a coffee cup).
- **Value of Silence** — Not every shot needs action or dialogue. A 3-second static close-up—character just breathing, blinking, existing—is often more powerful than any line of dialogue.

## I. Emotions Before the Camera — Faces, Eyes, Micro-expressions

> Emotions in live-action urban settings do not come from "prosthetic light effects" or "rendering parameters," but from the micro-expressions of real actors in front of the camera. The following mapping addresses the way the camera captures real people.

| Emotional Input | Facial Description | Eye Description | Micro-expression Supplement |
|---|---|---|---|
| Capable / Composed | Sharp expression, natural facial lines, not tense | Determined gaze, looking straight ahead, steady | Flat lip line, slightly tucked chin, calm and restrained aura |
| Sad / Desolate | Brows and eyes lowered, facial muscles relaxed | Gaze unfocused, looking out the window or at the ground | Corners of mouth naturally downturned, wistful expression, not forced |
| Gentle / Healing | Brows and eyes relaxed, soft facial features | Warm and focused gaze, as if listening intently | Corners of mouth naturally lifted slightly; not a smile, but has warmth |
| Stern / Guarded | Facial lines slightly taut, not exaggerated | Sharp gaze but not overdone, forceful focus | Chin tucked, restrained expression, defensive body language |
| Surprised / Touched | Outer corners of eyes raised, brows naturally lifted | Gaze suddenly focused, pupils slightly dilated | Mouth slightly open, vivid but not exaggerated expression |
| Pensive / Introspective | Detached expression, muscles around eyes relaxed | Gaze distant and unfocused, appearing to look without seeing | Calm expression, perhaps supporting chin with one hand or light finger tapping |
| Joyful / Lively | Natural laugh lines at corners of eyes, eyes slightly curved | Eyes bright and luminous, smile reaches the eyes | Mouth corners lifted, cheeks slightly flushed, vivid but not exaggerated |
| Tired / Weary | Eye sockets slightly sunken, overall facial relaxation | Hazy gaze, eyelids slightly heavy | Brows and eyes lowered, potential dark circles, relaxed expression |
| Anticipating / Longing | Brows and eyes slightly lifted, controlled facial activity | Eyes bright, light in pupils | Mouth corners slightly lifted, suppressed smile |
| Resolute / Determined | Clear facial lines, chin slightly raised | Gaze straight ahead, no avoidance | Chin tucked or slightly raised, expression bold but not aggressive |

## II. Light Before the Camera — Lighting Logic and Atmosphere

### 2.1 Natural Light Moments

| Time | Lighting Behavior | Color Temperature and Tone | Spatial Atmosphere |
|---|---|---|---|
| Early Morning | Low-angle warm white sunlight entering room through windows; outside, sunlight just crossing buildings, long shadows | 3500-4500K warm white, air slightly cold | Quiet, not yet awake, crisp morning mist, everything just beginning |
| Morning | Sunlight rising, cold white and clear, sharp shadows; exterior surface materials clear; indoor window light at peak area | 5000-6000K neutral-cold | Order established, daily operation, efficient and clear |
| Noon | Top light or near top light, shadows short and heavy; strong reflection on exterior materials; indoor window light entering from above | 5500-6500K cold tone | A sense of pause, midday gap, the peak of the daily routine |
| Afternoon | Sunlight shifting west, warm tones increasing, slanting light through windows or trees creating long shadows and light spots | 4000-5000K gradually warming | Lazy, time slowing down, the longest part of the day |
| Golden Hour | Extremely low-angle warm gold diffusion, extreme long shadows, everything coated in gold edges | 2800-3500K extremely warm | Fleeting preciousness, the only light source for warm memory segments |
| Blue Hour | After sun dips below horizon, sky deep blue/purple, natural light extremely dim and cold, artificial lights just turning on | 8000-10000K sky + warm artificial lights | Transitional, poetic, daytime ended, night not yet taken over |
| Late Night | Relying solely on artificial light—streetlamps, desk lamps, screen glow, headlights. Most areas submerged in shadows | Warm 2800-3200K / Cold 4000-6500K | Lonely or intimate, the world has quieted down |

### 2.2 Emotional Lighting

| Emotional Tone | Lighting Recipe | Photographic Constraints |
|---|---|---|
| Professional Restraint | Cold white window key light (5000-5500K), may be accompanied by cool screen fill on face, details kept in shadows | Moderate contrast ratio ~1:3, preserving spatial depth, glass partitions creating multi-layer reflections |
| Everyday Relaxation | Large-area window light diffusion (4500-5000K), softened by curtains, no hard shadows | Low contrast, translucent, shallow depth of field to highlight the subject |
| Cozy Home | Warm desk/floor lamp side light (2800-3200K), window light as auxiliary (if daytime), soft shadows | Warm wrapping, single warm point light source, shadows warm with detail |
| Urban Life | Mixed light—warm streetlamps (2800K) + cold shop white (4000K) + passing car headlights | Warm-dominated, cold light local contrast, handheld camera presence |
| Rainy Night Solitude | Wet ground reflecting streetlamps (cool base 6000K + warm 2800K spots), rain streaks on glass scattering light | Cold/warm coexistence, single warm light on face, rest submerged in cold darkness |
| Suspense / Tension | Hard side lighting, deep shadows, high light contrast, cold/warm contrast (e.g., cold blue outside window + warm yellow inside) | High contrast 1:8 or above, hard light outlining contours, shadows deep but detailed |
| Youthful Healing | Sufficient natural light (5000-5500K), large area diffusion, sky light + ground reflection fill | Low contrast, high key, clear and fresh, shallow depth of field softening background |
| Late Night Fragility | Single warm light source (desk lamp 2800-3200K or streetlamp outside window) creating an "island" of light | Minimalist light, shadows rich with detail, light ratio up to 1:8 |

## IV. Camera Grammar — Lens Choice and Movement

### 4.1 Focal Length Choice

| Focal Length | Narrative Character | Applicable Scenes |
|---|---|---|
| 24-28mm Wide | Envelops subject in environment, sense of spatial detachment, urban scale vs. individual smallness | Urban empty shots, rooftop overlook, lonely person in a crowd |
| 35mm Small Wide | Subjective viewpoint, documentary sense of "being there," coexistence of person and environment | Street walking, indoor conversation, daily following |
| 50mm Standard | Equivalent to human eye, objective and neutral, the most "honest" lens | Portraits, two-person dialogue, emotional gaze |
| 85mm Medium Tele | Compresses space, brings emotional distance closer, blurred background to highlight subject | Close-ups, watching from across the street, voyeuristic view |
| 135mm+ Tele | Separates subject from environment, extreme focus or surveillance | Isolated individual in city, distance viewing, narrative distance |

### 4.2 Shot Size Choice

| Shot Size | Framing Scope | Narrative Function |
|---|---|---|
| Extreme Close-up | Eye/Lip/Finger/Object detail | Emotional climax, key info reveal, sensory focus |
| Close-up | Top of head to chest | Emotional state, dialogue reaction, facial micro-expressions |
| Medium Close-up | Top of head to waist | Interaction, gestures, body language during dialogue |
| Medium Shot | Top of head to knees | Primary relationship between subject and space, multiple people in frame |
| Full Shot | Full body + partial environment | Subject's position in space, movement path |
| Long Shot / Empty | Full space, subject tiny or absent | Spatial narrative, emotional buffer, metaphorical breathing room |

### 4.3 Camera Movement

| Movement | Visual Psychology | Usage Scene |
|---|---|---|
| Fixed | Calm observation, letting time speak | Daily scenes, dialogue, empty "breathing" shots |
| Handheld (Shaky) | Presence, breathing, subjective intervention | Emotional fluctuation, street walking, intimate following |
| Stabilized Glide | Elegant city wandering, poetic | Transitions between important scenes, character entrances, spatial display |
| Slow Push | Attention gradually focusing, emotional proximity | Dialogue heating up, truth approaching, emotional progression |
| Slow Pull | Opening distance, revealing whole, emotional detachment | Parting, chapter conclusion, spatial display |
| Tracking | Walking with subject, subjective company | Walking in city, pursuit, daily following |
| Pan | Shifting focus, establishing spatial relations | Guiding viewer gaze, revealing relationship between spaces |

## I. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the director's core tool for converting a script into cinematic language. These constraints are tailored for Seedance 2.0 video generation; all lighting, movement, and spatial descriptions use concrete language executable by the model. Avoid abstract emotional summaries and render parameters that the model cannot process.

## II. The Iron Laws of Seedance 2.0 Description

> All descriptions in a live-action urban storyboard must adhere to the following translation principle: translate "director's intent" into "AI-executable physical instructions."

| Forbidden Abstract Expressions | Seedance 2.0 Concrete Replacements |
|---|---|
| She is very sad | Brows lowered, gaze unfocused and fixed on the floor, corners of the mouth naturally drooping, right hand unconsciously stroking the left wrist |
| The sunlight is nice | Afternoon sunlight entering from the left window at a 45° angle (approx. 4500K warm white), casting rectangular light patches of the window frame on the floor |
| The street is lively | Street-level shop windows glowing with warm yellow lights (approx. 3000K), five pedestrians walking slowly along the sidewalk, one pushing a stroller |
| He turns and leaves | Rotating slowly to the right by about 90 degrees, facing the right side of the frame, right foot steps forward about 0.6m first, followed by the left foot; entire action takes approx. 2 seconds |
| The atmosphere is oppressive | Single-source hard window light (cool white approx. 5000K) hitting at an angle; the rest of the room is deep, with faint contours visible, lighting ratio approx. 1:8 |
| Wind blows the curtains | White sheer curtains billowed by a breeze from the window about 15cm, then falling back, repeating in a cycle of approx. 2 seconds |

## III. Lighting and Atmosphere — Seedance 2.0 Physical Lighting Descriptions

### 3.1 Unified Lighting within a Scene
A single scene should not contain more than two core lighting schemes unless narrative-driven changes occur (e.g., someone turns on a desk lamp, sky light becomes brighter than indoor light after sunrise, moving from indoors to outdoors). Changes in light sources must be marked as trigger events in the storyboard.

### 3.2 Light Source Syntax (Required for every shot)
[Light Source] Key Light: {Light Type}, {Direction}, {Color Temperature K}, {Soft/Hard}
[Light Source] Fill/Ambient Light: {Light Type}, {Direction}, {Color Temperature K}
[Lighting Ratio] Approx. 1:{X}

### 3.3 Emotion → Lighting Matrix

| Emotion | Lighting Recipe (Directly enter into Seedance 2.0) | Visual Keywords |
|---|---|---|
| Workplace Restraint | Cool white key light (5000-5500K) side-entering from floor-to-ceiling window, cool screen light (6500K) face fill. Ratio ~1:3 | Neutral, cool, clear textures |
| Casual Relaxation | Large area diffused window light (5000K), softened by curtains. Ratio ~1:1.5, low contrast | Translucent, low-contrast, healing |
| Warm Intimacy | Warm desk lamp key light (2800-3200K), dark areas retain object outlines. Ratio ~1:4 | Warm-toned, wrapped, private |
| Urban Buzz | Mixed lighting — sodium street lamp warm yellow (2000-2200K) dominant, shop cool white (4000K) local contrast. Ratio ~1:5 | Warm-dominant, lively |
| Rainy Night Solitude | Warm yellow street lamp reflections on wet road (2800K), cool ambient (6000K sky diffusion), rain streaks scattering on glass. Ratio ~1:6 | Cold/warm coexistence, poetic solitude |
| Suspense/Tension | Side hard window light (cool white 5000K) single source, high contrast ~1:8, dark areas deep but with faint contours. Ratio ~1:8 | Oppressive, uncertain |
| Healing Rebirth | Sufficient natural diffuse light (5000-5500K), sky light + ground reflected fill. Ratio ~1:1.5 | High-key, translucent, hopeful |
| Late-night Fragility | Single warm source — desk lamp/street lamp outside (2800-3200K) forming "island" lighting, one side of face bright, one side dark. Ratio ~1:8 | Minimalist light, private, fragile |

### 3.4 Cold/Warm Tones and Narrative Stages
- **Cool Dominant** (5000K+): Workplace restraint, suspense/tension, cold solitude, rainy nights.
- **Warm Dominant** (2000-3500K): Warm intimacy, urban buzz, home life, golden hour.
- **Coexistence**: Transitional moments (Blue hour + lights turning on), rainy nights (cool environment + warm reflections).
- **Light Change = Narrative Signal**: Window sky changing from cool white to warm gold = passing of time; moving from office cool white to street warm yellow = scene and emotional shift.

### 3.5 Seedance 2.0 Lighting Adaptation Tips
- Color temperature digits help calibrate white balance: `Color temp approx. 3200K` is better than `warm light`.
- Lighting ratio digits help establish light/dark awareness: `Ratio approx. 1:4` is better than `soft shadows`.
- Light sources must have clear origins: `Entering from the left window at 45°` is better than `side lighting`.
- Describe reflection paths: `Wet road reflects warm yellow street lamp spots` is better than `ground has warm reflections`.

## IV. Environmental Dynamics — Making the Frame Breathe

### 4.1 Dynamic Density
Arrange at least one shot with environmental dynamics every 3-4 shots. Static dialogue scenes are no exception—at least one shot should feature rustling leaves outside, rising steam from a coffee cup, or curtains fluttering.

### 4.2 Urban Environmental Dynamic Elements (Seedance 2.0 Executable)

| Scene | Descriptive Environmental Dynamics |
|---|---|
| Indoors | Curtains billowed by breeze ~10cm then falling (2s cycle), steam slowly rising from coffee cup, insects flying within desk lamp illumination, car lights sweeping across ceiling |
| Street | Tree leaves rustling, pedestrians waiting at zebra crossing, bicycle riding through mid-ground, ripples in roadside puddles |
| Cafe/Restaurant | Coffee machine steam, light/shadow on window seat changing with clouds, barista wiping cups, wind chime ringing |
| Office | Blinds shadow moving with light angle, computer screen saver switching, water dispenser bubbling, printer ejecting paper |
| Late Night | Automatic convenience store door opening/closing, traffic lights casting red/green light, distant car headlights sweeping ceiling |
| Rooftop | Clothes on laundry line fluttering, skyline lights turning on/off, clouds moving slowly |

### 4.3 Seedance 2.0 Environmental Dynamic Specification
- Dynamics must have specific trajectories and speeds: `Leaves fluttering, 2-3 times per second` is better than `trees are moving`.
- Light dynamics must align with spatial logic: `When clouds block the sun, indoor window light area shrinks by 40%, lasting 3 seconds`.
- No baseless dynamics: No wind = no curtain movement. No open window = no wind.

## V. Character Action Rhythm — Physical Logic Concretization

### 5.1 Action Description Iron Law
All character actions must describe: **Trajectory + Speed/Duration + Body Part Coordination + Impact on Surrounding Objects**.

### 5.2 Daily Action Repository
*(Tables follow the logic of providing specific, actionable physical descriptions for actions like "Standing up," "Turning head," "Drinking coffee," etc.)*

### 5.3 Action Rhythm and Narrative Scenes
- **Daily/Dialogue**: Actions are steady and restrained. Label duration and trajectory for micro-actions. Rhythm should be relaxed, not "rushed."
- **Emotional Swings**: Increase action amplitude and speed slightly.
- **Conflict**: Actions should be crisp and decisive. Physical impact must be specific, e.g., "Right hand pushes the other's left shoulder, causing them to shift center of gravity backward by 20cm."
