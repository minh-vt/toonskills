# Realistic Urban Live-Action Style Guide

This style is specifically designed for "Realistic Urban Live-Action" themes. All art prompts, specifications, and generated content are strictly limited to the following:

- **Modern Worldview**: Set against a modern urban backdrop, including offices, cafes, homes, streets, shopping malls, etc., ensuring no absence of modern elements.
- **Realistic Live-Action**: All characters, scenes, and props are based on realistic live-action photography standards, emphasizing extreme details and authentic textures such as facial features, skin, hair, and material surfaces.
- **Urban Realistic Documentary**: Pursuing cinematic atmosphere on a realistic foundation, featuring strong contrast between light and shadow, rich details, and an overall ambiance that is straightforward, warm, and reflective of modern life.
- **Sweet Romance Atmosphere**: Suitable for showcasing gentle, healing, and romantic love stories, emphasizing emotional expression within a modern urban setting.

## Scope of Application

- AI art generation for realistic urban themes (characters, scenes, props, storyboards, etc.)
- Visual creations requiring extreme realism, delicate textures, and a modern urban atmosphere.

## Prohibited Content

- Cartoon, anime, illustration, or 2D styles
- Absence of modern elements, traditional/period costumes, or traditional architecture
- High-saturation fluorescent colors or neon colors
- Suggestive, revealing, explicit, or other inappropriate content

## Style Experience

With this style, you will experience:

- Characters with exquisite facial features and temperaments that are either gentle or professional; clothing, makeup, and hairstyles are all realistic to modern urban life.
- Scenes with rich spatial layers, ultra-clear textures, and an atmosphere that is authentic and full of life.
- Props such as smartphones, computers, and office supplies are all modeled on modern designs, featuring fine craftsmanship and premium textures.
- Storyboards and videos are based on realistic live-action, emphasizing narrative and emotional expression.

---

This style is only applicable to projects related to "Realistic Urban Live-Action." If other styles (such as traditional, illustration, anime, etc.) are required, please do not use this set of prompts.

# Character Base Image Generation · Urban Realism Constraint Manual

## I. Basic Character Principles

1. **Facial Features are the Soul** — The facial features are the sole anchor point for the character, rendered with pore-level precision.
2. **Character-Centric** — Basic clothing is determined by character description (identity/occupation/gender/scenario); subsequent specific costumes are added as layers.
3. **Four-View Consistency** — Facial features, physique, hairstyle, and basic clothing must be highly consistent across all views.
4. **Natural Realism** — The character's temperament (capable/gentle/cool/approachable) must be reflected even in a no-makeup state.
5. **Photographic Realism** — Using professional photography as an anchor, retain authentic skin texture (pores/micro-blemishes).

# Character Derivative Asset Generation · Urban Realistic Constraint Manual

---

## I. Overlay Principles

1.  **Facial Fidelity** — After the overlay, facial features must remain identical to the base model; facial shifting is strictly prohibited.
2.  **Pose Consistency** — Maintain the base model's natural standing posture; any changes to pose, movement, or body language are prohibited.
3.  **Layer-by-Layer Controllability** — Each layer is described independently to facilitate layer-specific replacement (e.g., changing clothing without altering makeup).
4.  **Style Uniformity** — All attire and grooming elements must conform to the same aesthetic system.
5.  **Quality Preservation** — The visual quality after overlay must not be lower than the base model.
6.  **Scope Limitation (Attire/Grooming Only)** — Only overlay makeup, hairstyles, clothing, and accessories; introducing props, scenes, environments, or actions is strictly prohibited.

---

## II. Overlay Layers

| Layer | Content | Description |
|---|---|---|
| L0 | Base Model | The foundation image; do not modify. |
| L1 | Makeup (Decision Layer) | Analyze user cues first, then determine intensity: "Basic/Light/Formal." |
| L2 | Hairstyling | Hair design + hair accessories. |
| L3 | Undergarments | Replace the basic white base layer. |
| L4 | Outerwear/Main Outfit | T-shirts, shirts, suits, jackets, dresses, etc. |

> **Scope Boundary**: Character derivative assets only include layers L0–L5 (attire and grooming). These belong to other asset categories.

# Urban Style Constraints · Live-Action Urban Realism · Technical Reference

---

## I. Color System and Visual Tone

- **Color Foundation** — The entire film is based on off-white (C1), warm skin tones (C2), and high-end gray (C6). The overall color temperature is warm (5200–5800K), with low-to-medium saturation (40–60%), presenting a warm and bright sense of urban life.
- **Warm-Cold Narrative Contrast** — Warm colors (amber C7, cream pink C5, metallic silver C8) serve as visual signals for emotional warming and relationship affirmation, while cold colors are used for segments involving stress, misunderstandings, or alienation. The switching between warm and cold should sync with the emotional arc.
- **Warm-Cold Distribution Strategy** — Daily routines and workplace segments should be primarily neutral/slightly cold, with warm colors reserved for intimate or reconciliation moments; avoid excessive use of warm tones throughout, which can lead to a collapse in emotional layering.
- **Color Palette First Principle** — Segment planning should bind emotional scenes (meetings/flirtation/dates/conflicts/reconciliations, etc.) before determining the primary/secondary colors and lighting schemes to avoid "plot-correct but tone-incorrect" results.
- **Forbidden Color Gamuts** — Highly saturated fluorescent colors, intense neon pollution, and excessive cyber-style aesthetics are incompatible with this style.

---

## II. Lighting Scheme System

- **Lighting as Narrative** — 6 lighting schemes correspond to different emotional segments. During the directorial planning stage, the lighting tone should be determined at the segment level rather than specified shot-by-shot.
- **Light Source Angle** — The default for front-facing shots is 30° oblique lighting (relatively soft), which is fine-tuned for side-facing shots based on the scene and character relationships.

| Lighting Scheme | Name | Color Tendency | Applicable Emotion |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Natural Diffusion | Off-white base + uniform soft light | Daily commute, slice-of-life narrative |
| B | Warm Side-Lighting | Creamy warm + localized warm light | Warming interactions, relaxed home life |
| C | Mixed Overhead/Ambient | Neutral gray + soft cold white | Workplace rhythm, rational communication |
| D | Window/Point Light | Amber warm dominant + cold blue background | Nighttime flirtation, intimate advancement |
| E | Street Neon Glow | Cold background + warm highlights | Urban night views, romantic wandering |
| F | Overcast Diffused Cold Light | Gray-blue tone + localized warm light | Transition segments, low points |

- **Warm-Cold Light Allocation** — Warm lights can be used throughout the film but require layered control. Reduce the proportion of warm lights in conflict/alienation segments to avoid clashes between emotions and lighting colors.
- **Atmospheric Mapping** — The atmospheric direction of every scene should be mapped to one of the aforementioned lighting schemes (A-F) to ensure visual consistency.

## III. Texture Direction

- **Ultra-Clear Documentary Feel** — The core of live-action realistic photography: visible skin texture, distinct individual strands of hair, and clear, believable material details.
- **High Contrast + Extreme Detail** — Acts as the anchor for visual texture. It is not "low-contrast aesthetic," nor "heavy-filter atmosphere," but a grounded, cinematic documentary feel.
- **Authentic Materials** — Set design, costumes, and props must have realistic credibility: suit fabrics, glass reflections, metal components, wooden furniture, and electronic screen highlights should all adhere to urban real-world logic.
- **Skin Texture** — Retain real pores and subtle blemishes; control the strength of skin smoothing to pursue a "realistic yet aesthetically pleasing" look.
- **Realism Does Not Mean Blandness** — Amplify emotional impact through composition, framing, and movement rhythm rather than relying on exaggerated effects or excessive post-processing.

---

## IV. Urban Scene Spatial Elements

Specific spatial elements in the urban worldview and their visual narrative functions:

- **Window Frames/Glass/Doorways** — Natural frame-in-frame composition tools that create spatial layering and a sense of distance in character relationships.
- **Streets/Subway/Elevators/Office Corridors** — Carriers of urban rhythm; the transition between commuting and pausing can directly map to a character's psychological state.
- **Cafés/Offices/Living Rooms** — Emotional containers: cafés imply probing, offices imply restraint, and homes imply safety and relaxation.
- **Weather and Time as Transitions** — Day-to-night, sunny-to-rainy, or dusk-to-night transitions can be used to buffer emotional shifts, avoiding hard cuts.
- **Visuals Over Explanations for Turning Points** — Prioritize visual means (sudden changes in framing, eye-line mismatches, spatial barriers) to handle emotional shifts, reducing the burden of exposition through dialogue.

---

## V. Urban Musical Instruments and Ambient Sounds

Constraints on sound elements within the urban worldview:

### Instrument Selection

- **Piano** — Core instrument for daily, heartwarming, and flashback segments, best at reflecting the texture of urban life.
- **Guitar** — An effective leader for relaxed, street-level, slow-paced romantic segments.
- **Strings** — The primary foundation for segments involving emotional elevation and outbursts.
- **Lo-fi/Light Electronic** — Style enhancers for youth-oriented, urban nightlife, or rhythm-shifting segments.
- **High Priority for Ambient Sound** — Real-world environmental sound is key to immersion in urban dramas; the musical score should not overpower the dialogue.

### Instrument Combination Strategy

| Emotional Stage | Instrument Combination |
|---|---|
| Stable/Opening/Closing | Piano solo or Guitar solo |
| Daily Progression | Piano + Light percussion |
| Flirtation/Romance | Piano + Strings |
| Emotional Outburst/Conflict | Strings dominant + Low-frequency base |
| Night Scene/Transition | Lo-fi/Light electronic + Ambient sound |

### Urban Ambient Sounds

- **Typical Ambient Sound Layers** — Traffic noise / Subway station arrival alerts / Elevator chimes / Coffee machine sounds / Keyboard typing / Rain / AC humming / Distant urban white noise.
- **Label 1–2 core ambient sounds for every scene** to assist in subsequent sound design. The more accurate the ambient sound layers, the stronger the urban realistic texture.

# Urban Storyboard Constraints · Live-Action Urban Realism · Technical Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Positioning

The storyboard is the core tool for directors to translate a script into cinematic language. The following provides constraints and specifications specifically for the live-action urban realism style within the storyboard framework.

---

## II. Lighting and Atmosphere

- **Lighting Consistency within Scenes** — A single scene should not contain more than two lighting schemes unless there is a clear narrative pivot (e.g., turning off a lamp → cold moonlight).
- **Emotional Palette Binding** — Each scene must be tied to at least one emotional state (e.g., heartbeat encounter / work conflict / sweet date), and primary/secondary color continuity must be maintained throughout the shot sequence.
- **Light Source Angle Specifications** — The default light source for frontal shots is a 30° oblique angle (softer). Side shots should be adjusted based on the requirements of the frame. When describing light and shadow, the difference between frontal and side angles must be specified.
- **Color Temperature and Narrative Phase Matching** — Maintain neutral/cold tones for work/solitude/conflict segments; warm tones (warm orange + cold blue background contrast) should only be used after emotional escalation.
- **Lighting Transitions as a High-Level Technique** — Transitioning from natural light (A) to indoor warm light (B) equals the passage of time from day to night. Mark points of lighting change clearly in the storyboard.

---

## III. Environmental Dynamics

- **Environmental Dynamics for "Breathing Room"** — Flowing car lights outside the window, flickering computer screens, steam from coffee cups, phone screens lighting up. At least one shot in every 3-4 shots should include environmental dynamics to prevent the image from appearing "static."
- **Urban Environmental Elements First** — Environmental dynamics should utilize elements within the urban world view: flowing headlights, raindrops on windows, rising steam, phone screen flicker, swaying curtains, elevator indicator counts. Elements incompatible with a modern urban setting are prohibited.

---

## IV. Urban Movement Rhythm

- **Urban Movements Must Be Natural** — All character movements follow a natural rhythm by default. Standing up, turning, and raising hands should all be labeled as "natural" or "casual."
- **Prop Interaction** — The use of props such as mobile phones, coffee cups, documents, and car keys is a natural dynamic asset in urban settings. Include details of prop interaction in the shot descriptions to enhance the sense of everyday life.
- **Modernized Body Language** — Urban character behavior should conform to modern urban sensibilities: confident gait, casual leaning, and involuntary micro-movements (tucking hair, checking watch, biting lip).

---

## V. Camera Movement Prohibitions

- **Prohibit Fast Camera Movements** — Whip pans, rapid zooms, and handheld shaking conflict with the essence of live-action urban realism (unless in action/chase scenes).
- **Prohibit Fancy Transitions** — Wipes, spins, blinds, etc., are incompatible with this style.
