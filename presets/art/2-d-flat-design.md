# Flat Design Style Guide

This style is specifically tailored for the "Flat Design" theme. All art prompts, specifications, and generated content are strictly limited to the following:

- **Flat Design Style**: Focused on flat design principles, covering UI design, infographics, illustrations, etc., to embody a modern minimalist aesthetic.
- **Solid Color Texture**: All characters, scenes, and props must adhere to the 2D flat style standard, emphasizing solid color fills, no shadows, no gradients, and clean lines.
- **Minimalist Modern Aesthetic**: Building on the flat style to pursue clear expression, with bright and concise color palettes, simplified details, and an overall atmosphere that is minimalist, modern, and pleasant.
- **Emotional Expression**: Suitable for showcasing gentle, healing, and romantic flat-style content, emphasizing emotional expression within a modern minimalist framework.

## Scope of Application

- AI art generation for 2D flat-style subjects (characters, scenes, props, storyboards, etc.)
- Visual creations requiring an ultra-flat texture, clean lines, and a minimalist modern atmosphere.

## Prohibited Content

- Realistic rendering, 3D rendering, and photorealism
- Shadows, gradients, textures, and lighting effects
- Complex details, intricate textures, and realistic backgrounds
- Dark, violent, or adult-oriented content

## Style Experience

Under this style, you will experience:

- Characters with minimalist facial features and fresh dispositions, with shapes and clothing designed in a 2D flat style.
- Scenes with concise spatial layers, clear and fluid lines, and an atmosphere that is authentic yet modern.
- Props, daily objects, and environmental details designed with flattened shapes, simple craftsmanship, and a crisp texture.
- Storyboards and visuals based on 2D flat design, emphasizing narrative and emotional expression.

---

This style is only applicable to projects related to "Flat Design." For other styles (such as photorealistic 3D, digital illustration, modern 3D, etc.), please do not use this set of prompts.

## I. Basic Principles

1. **Outline is the Soul** — Lines are the anchor of the character, constructed by layered flat color blocks.
2. **Character-Centric** — Basic attire is determined by the character description (identity/occupation/gender/scenario); specific costumes/accessories are added as overlay layers later.
3. **Four-View Consistency** — Outlines, body type, hairstyle, and basic clothing must be highly consistent across all views.
4. **Color Block Expression** — No gradients or shadows; use color block contrast to express depth.

# Character Derivative Asset Generation · Flat Style Constraint Manual

---

## I. Overlay Principles

1. **Consistent Silhouette** — The outline after the overlay must be perfectly identical to the base model; silhouette shifting is prohibited.
2. **Fixed Pose** — Maintain the base model's natural standing posture; any changes in pose, action, or body language are prohibited.
3. **Layer-by-Layer Control** — Each layer is described independently to facilitate layer-specific replacement (change costume without changing makeup).
4. **Unified Style** — All clothing and grooming elements must adhere to the same flat aesthetic system.
5. **Color Block Integrity** — The standard of color blocks after overlaying must not be lower than that of the base model.
6. **Costume & Grooming Category Only** — Only overlay makeup, hairstyles, clothing, and accessories; introducing props, scenes, environments, or actions is prohibited.

---

| Layer | Content | Description |
|---|---|---|
| L0 | Base Model | Basic character model; do not modify. |

## III. Makeup Constraints (L1)

### Base Model to Derivative Makeup Strategy (Key)

> Although the character base model is "no-makeup," derivative assets must enter the makeup styling process by default. The system should analyze the needs based on user clues and decide the intensity between Basic, Soft, and Formal makeup rather than keeping the "no-makeup" look.

### L1 Clue Analysis and Makeup Decision

| Step | Processing Content | Decision Result |
|---|---|---|
| S1 | Extract user clues: Facial state words, emotional words, intensity words. | Formulate makeup requirement summary. |
| S2 | Filter non-makeup clues: Props/scenes/actions/pose words are not used as a basis for makeup. | Prevent misjudgment. |
| S3 | Match makeup style matrix and assign intensity level. | Basic Makeup / Soft Makeup / Formal Makeup. |
| S4 | Generate final L1 prompt. | Output the conclusion only; do not output the analysis process. |

### Clue-to-Makeup Mapping (Execution Standard)

| Clue Type | Typical Clues | L1 Decision |
|---|---|---|
| No obvious facial emphasis | Only clothing/hair changes; no emotion or state emphasized. | Basic Makeup |

# Flat Design Constraints · 2D Flat Design · Technical Reference

---

## I. Color System and Visual Tone

- **Color Foundation** — The entire piece is based on Bright Blue (C1), Energetic Orange (C2), and Cream White (C9). The overall color temperature is neutral (5500-6500K) with mid-to-high saturation (70-90%), presenting the fresh and clean aesthetic of modern flat design.
- **Warm/Cool Narrative Contrast** — Warm colors (Energetic Orange C2, Warm Pink C7) serve as visual signals for emotional escalation, used locally to reinforce moments of infatuation or romance. Cool colors (Bright Blue C1, Pale Purple C6) are used for daily life and nighttime sequences. Transitions between warm and cool should be synchronized with the story arc rather than mixed arbitrarily.
- **Warm/Cool Distribution Strategy** — Daily/office sequences maintain a neutral-to-cool color base; warm colors are released only after emotional escalation, using the contrast to create narrative turning points.
- **Palette-First Principle** — Paragraph planning must first bind the emotional scene (Daily/Infatuation/Romance/Flashback, etc.), then determine the main color, secondary color, and color block scheme to avoid "plot-appropriate but emotion-inappropriate" coloring.
- **Forbidden Color Gamut** — High-saturation fluorescent colors, neon colors, and 3D rendering color systems are incompatible with this style.

---

## II. Color Block Scheme System

- **Color Blocks as Narrative** — There are 6 color block schemes corresponding to different emotional sequences. At the director planning stage, the baseline for color block direction should be determined at the sequence level, rather than specified shot-by-shot.
- **No Traditional Lighting in Flat Design** — Create "pseudo-lighting" through brightness differences between color blocks; do not use light source angles.

| Color Block Scheme | Scheme Name | Tone Tendency | Applicable Emotion |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Solid Block | Warm Orange + Cream White + Light Blue | Daily/Warm |
| B | Block Contrast | Warm Pink + Warm Orange Contrast | Infatuation, Escalation |
| C | Cool Tone | Cool Blue + Grey + White | Office/Study, Calm |
| D | Warm-Dominated | Warm Orange + Warm Pink + Light Yellow | Romantic, Sweet |
| E | Cool-Dominated | Dark Blue + Purple + Warm Yellow accents | Night/Quiet |
| F | Low Saturation | Light Yellow + Light Grey + Warm Pink | Memory/Flashback |

- **Warm/Cool Distribution** — Warm color blocks (Energetic Orange/Warm Pink) are suitable for emotional escalation sequences; calm/nighttime sequences use cool color blocks. The director may adjust the warm/cool switching point according to narrative needs.
- **Atmospheric Mapping** — The atmospheric direction of each scene should be mappable to one of the above color block schemes (A-F) to ensure visual consistency.

---

## III. Texture Direction

- **Geometric Minimalism** — The core of flat design: solid color blocks, clear outlines, no shadows, no gradients, and well-defined boundaries.
- **Clean Lines + Pure Colors** — This is the anchor of the visual texture. It is not about realistic rendering or complex details, but rather a geometric and minimalist expression.
- **No Realistic Elements Allowed** — All visual elements must adhere to the flat style: no shadows, no gradients, no textures, no lighting effects. "3D feel" and "realistic feel" are prohibited.
- **Clear Block Contrast** — Boundaries between color blocks are distinct; the contrast between main and secondary colors is clear to avoid blurred transitions.
- **Flat Does Not Mean Monotonous** — Convey emotion through color contrast and compositional design rather than relying on realistic details. Use color block size and hue variation to create visual layers.

---

## IV. Flat Design Spatial Elements

Specific spatial elements of the flat style and their visual narrative functions:

- **Solid Backgrounds/Color Zone Partitioning** — A natural carrier for composition; use color zones to divide spatial levels, creating a clean sense of space and visual depth.
- **Geometric Shapes/Icons** — The ideographic tools of flat design: Circle = Warm/Complete, Triangle = Dynamic/Conflict, Square = Stable/Daily.
- **Negative Space** — An important emotional device in flat design. Large areas of negative space = Focus, Full blocks = Richness, Partial negative space = Breathing room.
- **Solid Color Transitions Between Sequences** — This style has a wealth of color block assets (different hues/brightness variants). It is recommended to use solid color blocks as emotional buffers for sequence transitions rather than hard cuts.
- **Color Block Changes for Turning Points** — Prioritize visual means (brightness changes, hue shifts, abrupt color block contrast) rather than relying on dialogue for explanation.

---

## V. Flat Design Instruments and Ambient Sound

Constraints on audio elements in the flat style:

### Instrument Selection

- **Electronic Synthesizers** — The core instrument for daily/warm/modern sequences, best at expressing the clean feel of flat design.
- **Piano** — For romantic/emotional sequences; the clean timbre fits a minimalist atmosphere.
- **Light/Upbeat Tempo** — For bright/energetic sequences; suitable for fast-paced scenes.
- **Strings** — Laying the foundation for emotional escalation; should not overshadow other elements.

### Instrument Combination Strategy

| Emotional Stage | Instrument Combination |
|---|---|
| Stable/Opening/Closing | Solo electronic effects |
| Daily/Warm | Synth + Light Piano |
| Emotional Escalation/Romance | Piano + Strings |
| Key Emotional Moment | Ambient sound only (no soundtrack) |

### Flat Design Ambient Sound

- **Typical Ambient Layers** — City traffic sounds / Office keyboard sounds / Cafe background noise / Curtains blowing in the wind / Crowd murmurs / Mouse clicks.
- **Mark 1-2 core ambient sounds for each scene** to assist with subsequent sound design. The richer the layers of ambient sound, the more immersive the scene will be.

# Storyboard Table Flat Design Constraints · 2D Flat Design · Technique Reference

---

## I. Storyboard Table Positioning

The storyboard table is the core tool for directors to translate a script into lens language. The following provides specific constraint standards for the 2D flat design style within the storyboard.

---

## II. Color and Atmosphere

- **Color Consistency within a Scene** — No more than two color schemes should appear in a single scene, unless there is a clear narrative shift (e.g., a sudden switch from warm color blocks to cold color blocks).
- **Emotional Palette Binding** — Each scene must be tied to at least one emotional setting (e.g., daily warmth/heart-fluttering moment/romantic scene/quiet night), maintaining continuity of primary + secondary colors across the shot group.
- **Color Contrast Standards** — Medium contrast is the default; high/low contrast must be specified in the description.
- **Matching Warm/Cold Tones with Narrative Stages** — Daily sequences should maintain a warm base tone, while cold tones are reserved for nighttime or emotional shifts.
- **Color Transitions as an Advanced Technique** — Graduating from warm to cold = emotional transformation. Mark the specific point of color change in the storyboard table.

---

## III. Color Block Dynamics

- **Color Block Variations Enhance Visual "Breathability"** — Use variations in hue, brightness, and scale. Arrange at least one shot with color block changes every 3–4 shots to prevent the visual from appearing "dead."
- **Prioritize Flat Design Environmental Elements** — Color block dynamics should utilize elements consistent with the flat style: hue gradients, color block displacement, brightness transitions, and geometric shape changes. Realistic or 3D elements are prohibited.

---

## IV. Flat Design Action Pacing

- **Action Must Be Concise** — All character movements default to a natural pace. Actions like raising a hand or turning around should be labeled as "concise" or "smooth."
- **No More Than Two Actions per Single Shot** — "Raise hand + smile" is acceptable; "Raise hand + smile + turn around + walk" will result in a breakdown of the style.
- **Use Suggestion for Sweet/Romantic Interactions** — Eye contact, hand gestures, or passing objects. Do not write large-scale dual-character interactions such as "hugging" or "kissing."

---

## V. Prohibited Camera Movements

- **Complex Camera Movements are Prohibited** — Rotation, distortion, and rapid switching conflict with the temperament of this style.
- **Fancy Transitions are Prohibited** — Rotation, distortion, wipes, etc., are incompatible with this style.
- **Static by Default** — More than 70% of shots should be from a fixed camera position, allowing the color blocks and composition of the frame to speak for themselves.
